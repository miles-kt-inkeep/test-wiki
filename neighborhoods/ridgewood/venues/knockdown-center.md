# Knockdown Center

Knockdown Center is a 50,000-square-foot multi-room concert and dance venue at 52-19 Flushing Avenue, on the [Maspeth–Ridgewood](../overview.md) border in Queens. The building is a former door factory — specifically, the Knockdown Manufacturing Company plant, which produced flat-pack ("knockdown") doors and door-related hardware from the 1903 founding through the 1980s. The space was redeveloped beginning in 2012 by founder Tyler Myers and reopened as a music-and-arts venue in April 2014. It has since become the largest single live-music venue in Queens and one of the city's most-booked warehouse-scale rooms.

## History

### 1903–1980s — the door factory

The building at 52-19 Flushing Avenue was built in stages between 1903 and the 1930s as a glass and door manufacturing plant. The Knockdown Manufacturing Company, founded in 1903 in Long Island City and relocated to the Flushing Avenue site in 1906, became one of the largest door manufacturers in the Northeast, supplying hardware to commercial and residential developers from the 1910s through the 1960s. The factory closed in the early 1980s; the building was used through the 1990s and 2000s for various industrial and storage tenants.

### 2012–2014 — the redevelopment

In 2012 Tyler Myers (a real-estate developer with prior work in arts-space conversions) bought the building and began converting the main 50,000-square-foot factory floor into a multi-purpose event space, with smaller back-room spaces for galleries, programming offices, and a forthcoming dance room. The conversion preserved much of the original industrial structure — exposed steel beams, the original sawtooth glass roof, and the long rectangular floor plan — while adding HVAC, sound systems, modern egress, and a programming bar. Knockdown Center opened in April 2014 with a series of art installations and small concerts.

### 2014–2018 — the warehouse-art-and-concert period

Through its first four years Knockdown Center programmed a hybrid mix of art installations (Sarah Sze and several other emerging-to-mid-career contemporary artists used the main hall for large-format works), small-to-mid-size concerts (often booked through external promoters), and occasional rave events. The venue's all-ages permitting and 1,500-to-2,500-capacity flex (depending on configuration) made it the rare New York space that could host a Friday-night techno rave and a Sunday-afternoon family concert without renegotiating its operating permit each time.

### 2018–present — the dance-venue period and outdoor expansion

In 2018 Knockdown Center installed its first major outdoor courtyard programming — "The Yard," a 10,000-square-foot outdoor space along the back of the building — and pivoted bookings toward a higher percentage of dance and electronic music. The Black Madonna (now The Blessed Madonna), Honey Dijon, Avalon Emerson, Octo Octa, DVS1, and the broader underground roster the Bushwick and Ridgewood dance scene had been building became Knockdown Center anchors. In 2022 the venue added "The Ruins" — a programmed extension of the outdoor space using an adjacent former lumber yard — for warehouse-rave-scale events with capacities approaching 3,500. The 2023–2024 program included Avant Gardner / Brooklyn Mirage-scale weekend rave events.

The venue's smaller indoor back room ("The Theater," roughly 350-capacity) hosts concerts of indie and smaller dance acts; the main hall and outdoor courtyards host the larger bookings.

## Building and layout

The main hall is a roughly 22,000-square-foot rectangular open floor with exposed steel beams and a sawtooth-glass roof; capacity is typically 1,500–2,500 standing depending on stage configuration. The Theater is the smaller back-room indoor space (350 capacity). The Yard is the outdoor courtyard (about 10,000 square feet, weather-dependent, summer-active). The Ruins is the 2022 outdoor expansion using the adjacent lumber yard (3,500 capacity).

The venue sits at the dead end of Flushing Avenue near the Brooklyn-Queens border; the closest subway is the L at Wyckoff Avenue (15-minute walk) or the M at Forest Avenue (10-minute walk). The neighborhood is industrial — most of the surrounding blocks are working factories and warehouses — which has been a precondition for the venue's noise and operating tolerances.

## Place in the wiki

Knockdown Center is the contemporary Queens-side answer to [Brooklyn Steel](../../bushwick/venues/brooklyn-steel.md): a large, flexible, industrially-zoned room that has become the borough's institutional dance flagship. It functions alongside the Brooklyn Mirage (East Williamsburg, the city's largest open-air dance venue), [Nowadays](./nowadays.md), and Public Records (Gowanus) as one of the four anchors of the post-2015 New York dance-venue circuit. Its all-ages and hybrid art-concert programming distinguish it from the comparable Brooklyn-side rooms.
