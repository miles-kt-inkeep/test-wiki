# Nowadays

Nowadays is an indoor-and-outdoor dance venue at 56-06 Cooper Avenue, on the Queens side of the [Ridgewood](../overview.md) / [Bushwick](../../bushwick/overview.md) border. The venue was opened in summer 2015 by Eamon Harkin and Justin Carter — the co-founders of the long-running Mister Saturday Night and Mister Sunday day-and-evening party series. Through its first decade Nowadays has become one of the most-cited examples of the "listening-room dance" template — long DJ sets, Funktion-One sound systems, room-EQ-tuned acoustics, no-phone-on-the-dance-floor norms — that has reshaped New York and international club culture in the late 2010s and 2020s.

## History

### 2008–2014 — Mister Saturday Night

Eamon Harkin and Justin Carter began running the Mister Saturday Night party in 2008 — initially at a series of one-night-only spaces, then at Studio B in Greenpoint, then at multiple Brooklyn warehouse venues. By 2010 the party had a national-press reputation as the city's most respected underground dance event; its sister Mister Sunday day-party (sunset-time outdoor DJ sets, started 2009) ran in Brooklyn parks and the back gardens of Williamsburg bars. Through 2014 the pair had built audience, booking infrastructure, and a sound-and-curation aesthetic — but no permanent venue.

### 2015 — Nowadays opens as outdoor garden bar

In summer 2015 Harkin and Carter opened Nowadays at 56-06 Cooper Avenue, in a former parking lot adjacent to a Ridgewood warehouse building. The initial venue was outdoor-only — a fenced-in garden bar with a small DJ booth, picnic-style seating, food and drink service, and Saturday-only programming. Mister Sunday relocated to the new outdoor space; Mister Saturday Night continued running occasional events.

### 2017 — indoor space opens; year-round operation

In November 2017 Nowadays expanded into the adjacent warehouse building, opening a 350-capacity indoor dance space with a Funktion-One sound system, room-EQ-tuned acoustics, and a separate listening lounge. The expansion made the venue year-round and added Friday and Saturday night programming to the existing Sunday day-party schedule.

### 2018–present — institutional Nowadays

From 2018 onward Nowadays has been the city's most-influential post-Output dance venue. The booking philosophy emphasizes long DJ sets (six-, eight-, twelve-hour sets are common), house and disco-leaning underground programming, and resident-DJ slots that turn over slowly. The venue's no-phone-on-the-dance-floor norm (a sticker is applied to each guest's phone camera at the door) was widely covered in *The New York Times*, *Mixmag*, and *Resident Advisor* through the late 2010s as the canonical example of the "listening-room" dance shift. The model has been copied by other venues internationally — Berlin's smaller post-Berghain rooms, London's Brilliant Corners, and various Asian and South American clubs have all cited Nowadays as an explicit reference.

The venue's residents (a slowly rotating roster including the founders themselves, Aurora Halal, Carlos Souffront, Daphna, Anthony Naples, and DJ Python among others) anchor the year-round program; visiting bookings have included Theo Parrish, Moodymann, Floating Points, Octo Octa, Honey Dijon, and a long roster of European underground residents.

## Building and layout

The indoor space is roughly 350-capacity — a converted warehouse front room with the dance floor centered on the Funktion-One stack, a bar at the back, and a smaller listening lounge off to one side. The outdoor space is roughly 600-capacity warm-weather — picnic seating, a smaller secondary sound system, and food and drink service. The combined indoor-outdoor configuration accommodates around 900–1,000 across both spaces. Closest subway is the M at Fresh Pond Road (10-minute walk) or the L at Halsey (15-minute walk).

## Place in the wiki

Nowadays sits in the same conceptual slot for the post-2015 Queens-Brooklyn warehouse scene that the [Paradise Garage](../../soho/venues/paradise-garage.md) occupied for the late-1970s SoHo disco scene — a single venue whose curatorial discipline, sound system, and residential-DJ culture define a generational shift in how dance music is presented and consumed. The Manhattan venue most often compared to Nowadays is Public Records (Gowanus, technically Brooklyn), which opened in 2019 with an explicitly Nowadays-influenced model. The venue functions alongside [Knockdown Center](./knockdown-center.md) and Brooklyn Mirage as part of the Queens-and-Brooklyn dance circuit, though its smaller capacity and listening-room aesthetic distinguish its niche from either.
