# Trans-Pecos

Trans-Pecos is a DIY all-ages performance space at 915 Wyckoff Avenue, [Ridgewood](../overview.md), Queens, directly on the Brooklyn-Queens border across from [Bushwick](../../bushwick/overview.md). The venue opened in late 2014 as a co-founded project of several Silent Barn alumni — most prominently the curator and event organizer Todd Pendu — and through the back half of the 2010s and into the 2020s it has anchored the smaller-scale, all-ages, art-and-noise end of the city's warehouse-show ecosystem. The venue's role overlaps with the Silent Barn lineage (the long-running Bushwick DIY space that closed in 2018) and the Bushwick all-ages tradition more broadly.

## History

### 2014 — opening

Trans-Pecos opened in November 2014 in a former auto-repair shop at 915 Wyckoff Avenue, a one-story corner building at the intersection of Wyckoff and Cypress avenues. The founders (a collective rather than a single owner, with Todd Pendu in the public-facing curatorial role for the early years) configured the space as a flexible performance room with no fixed stage, removable seating, and a small back-yard outdoor area. From opening night the venue programmed an all-ages calendar mixing experimental music, noise, indie rock, poetry, and a continuing series of curatorial events that overlapped with the experimental-classical and downtown-art-music traditions out of Manhattan.

### 2015–2018 — the Silent Barn handoff

Silent Barn — the long-running DIY space first opened in Ridgewood in 2006 and relocated to Bushwick in 2012 — closed in April 2018 after years of financial and organizational difficulty. Through the 2015-to-2018 period Trans-Pecos gradually inherited much of the audience, the curatorial habits, and several specific event series that Silent Barn had run; many touring bands that would have played Silent Barn in 2014 played Trans-Pecos in 2018. The handoff was not formal — the two venues had different ownership structures and curatorial teams — but the cultural continuity was widely recognized.

### 2019–present — institutional Trans-Pecos

Through 2019 and the 2020s Trans-Pecos has become one of the city's most consistently active small all-ages spaces. The programming continues the pattern of the early years — experimental music, noise, indie rock, poetry, and curatorial events — with notable through-lines including the Pendu Sound Recordings (in-house cassette label) catalog and recurring residencies from local experimental composers. The venue's small capacity (roughly 100–150 standing) and all-ages permitting structure mean that nationally-touring bands of any commercial scale book it as a Bushwick-overflow show rather than a flagship date; the venue's value sits in the continuity and density of its small-scale weekly programming.

The 2020–2021 pandemic period reduced indoor programming significantly; the venue resumed full programming in 2022. The Silent Barn closure has been the most-cited counterfactual in the venue's institutional history — Trans-Pecos has, by the late 2020s, run for longer than Silent Barn did at any single address.

## Building and layout

The venue is a single-room corner space, roughly 100–150 capacity, with a small bar, removable seating, no fixed stage, and a small back-yard. The closest subway is the L at Halsey (5-minute walk) or the M at Forest Avenue (10-minute walk). The neighborhood is mixed industrial-residential; the venue sits on a corner facing the residential Cypress Avenue side and the industrial Wyckoff Avenue side. Like [Knockdown Center](./knockdown-center.md) the venue's permitting and operating tolerances depend on the surrounding industrial zoning.

## Place in the wiki

Trans-Pecos sits in the same conceptual slot for the 2014-and-after Queens-Brooklyn border warehouse scene that Silent Barn occupied for the 2006–2018 Bushwick scene — a small, curated, all-ages, art-noise-leaning room with deep ties to a curatorial collective. It functions alongside larger Ridgewood neighbors [Knockdown Center](./knockdown-center.md) and [Nowadays](./nowadays.md) as the small-capacity end of the borough's contemporary warehouse circuit.
