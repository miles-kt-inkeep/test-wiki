# Ridgewood

Ridgewood is the Queens neighborhood directly across the Brooklyn-Queens border from [Bushwick](../bushwick/overview.md), and since roughly 2014 it has functioned as the third Brooklyn-warehouse-scene neighborhood — the place every dance event, indie band, or DIY night moved when Bushwick's industrial blocks priced out. The borough boundary runs through the middle of the Bushwick / Ridgewood music geography along Wyckoff and Cypress avenues; almost no one going to a show distinguishes between the two sides. But on a wiki-borough frame Ridgewood is one of the few Queens neighborhoods with a fully built-out late-2010s and 2020s live-music story, anchored by three rooms — [Knockdown Center](./venues/knockdown-center.md), [Nowadays](./venues/nowadays.md), and [Trans-Pecos](./venues/trans-pecos.md) — that between them carry the majority of the Queens-side warehouse circuit.

## Geography and character

Ridgewood sits along Myrtle Avenue between Cypress Hills Cemetery to the east and Bushwick to the west, with Wyckoff Avenue marking the borough line. The M train (Wyckoff Av, Halsey, Forest Av, Fresh Pond Rd) is the spine; the L stops at Wyckoff one block into Brooklyn but functionally serves Ridgewood as much as Bushwick. The neighborhood is largely working-class (historically German, then Polish, more recently Latino and Eastern European) with a long-running industrial loft district along Flushing Avenue at the Maspeth border — most of the late-2010s music venues are in that industrial strip.

The Maspeth-Ridgewood-Bushwick triangle (sometimes loosely called "East Williamsburg" by promoters, though technically the warehouse blocks are split across the three neighborhoods) carries a remarkable concentration of music-relevant industrial real estate. Knockdown Center occupies a former door factory; Nowadays a former parking lot and warehouse; Trans-Pecos a former auto shop. The neighborhood's combination of intact industrial buildings, late-1900s residential stock, M and L train access, and looser noise/zoning enforcement than in Williamsburg or DUMBO has been the precondition.

## Music history

### Pre-2010 — almost no music story (in this wiki's frame)

Ridgewood's pre-2010 music geography is almost entirely off this wiki's frame. The neighborhood had a long German-American polka and oompah tradition through midcentury, a Yugoslavian-immigrant scene in the 1970s and 1980s, and a quieter Latino dance-music presence from the 1990s onward — but none of this sits inside the rock / dance / hip-hop / jazz frame the wiki is built around. Parquet Courts (the indie band that broke in 2012 with *Light Up Gold*) lived in a Ridgewood loft through their formation years; the band frequently identified as "Ridgewood" rather than "Bushwick" in early press, which marked the very beginning of the borough-line shift.

### 2014–2017 — the first wave: Trans-Pecos, Nowadays-pre-warehouse, Knockdown Center

Three things happened roughly in parallel between 2014 and 2017. **Trans-Pecos** opened in late 2014 at 915 Wyckoff Avenue, taking on much of the role Silent Barn had played in Bushwick before its 2018 closure — a DIY all-ages space programming punk, noise, experimental, and small-band rock. **Knockdown Center** opened in April 2014 at 52-19 Flushing Avenue (technically Maspeth, but Ridgewood-adjacent and Ridgewood-promoted) — a 50,000-square-foot former door factory that immediately became the city's largest non-permitted-but-zoning-tolerated warehouse-show space. **Nowadays** opened in summer 2015 at 56-06 Cooper Avenue, initially as a Saturday-only outdoor garden bar with a small DJ booth, gradually expanding through the back half of the decade into a year-round indoor-and-outdoor dance space.

The first wave of Ridgewood programming was a generational handoff: the bookings and the audience were the same people who had been going to Output, Bossa Nova Civic Club, and the various Bushwick warehouse spaces, now displaced one M-train stop east. The press economy treated Ridgewood as "Bushwick continued" through this entire period.

### 2017–2022 — institutionalization

By 2017 the three Ridgewood venues had become the city's most-booked warehouse-scale rooms. Nowadays added a second indoor space in 2017 (the same year Brooklyn Steel opened over the border in Bushwick) and began running Mister Saturday Night and Mister Sunday day-parties as institutional weekend bookings. Knockdown Center expanded its outdoor courtyard and added a smaller back-room space; it began programming weekly Saturday-night warehouse-rave events. The neighborhood's transit pattern locked in — most of the audience was coming from Manhattan and Brooklyn on the M and L, not from the Queens side.

In the late 2010s and early 2020s several smaller venues filled out the geography. Mood Ring (technically Bushwick, but Wyckoff-adjacent) and Public Records (Gowanus, but frequently programmed alongside the Ridgewood rooms) created a four-venue dance circuit that the city's underground dance economy has built around.

### 2023–today — Knockdown Center's outdoor expansion, the post-pandemic recovery

Knockdown Center's outdoor expansion (the "Ruins" courtyard programming from 2022 and the Avant Gardner-style multi-room rave program from 2023) put it functionally on par with the Brooklyn Mirage in East Williamsburg as a city-flagship rave space. The post-pandemic recovery brought all three Ridgewood venues back to capacity by 2023; Nowadays in particular has become a national-press touchstone for "the New York listening room" sound — long sets, room-EQ'd Funktion-One systems, no phone usage on the dance floor.

## Key venues

- [Knockdown Center](./venues/knockdown-center.md) — 52-19 Flushing Avenue, Maspeth / Ridgewood; the 50,000-square-foot converted door factory.
- [Nowadays](./venues/nowadays.md) — 56-06 Cooper Avenue, Ridgewood; the indoor-and-outdoor dance venue defining the city's current listening-room culture.
- [Trans-Pecos](./venues/trans-pecos.md) — 915 Wyckoff Avenue, Ridgewood; the DIY space inheriting the Silent Barn lineage.

## Notable artists and acts

Ridgewood is more venue-shaped than band-shaped, like neighboring [Bushwick](../bushwick/overview.md). Parquet Courts (whose early biography ran through Ridgewood lofts before broadly identifying as Brooklyn) is the most-cited band of the neighborhood. The DJ community that built Nowadays and Mister Saturday Night — Eamon Harkin, Justin Carter, Aurora Halal, and the underground roster around Mutual Dreaming — has anchored the post-2015 residential-DJ scene, though most members live across the Brooklyn line.

## Today

Ridgewood in 2026 remains the most active warehouse-dance neighborhood inside Queens; the three flagship rooms continue to draw audiences from Manhattan, Brooklyn, and increasingly Queens itself. Gentrification has come to the residential blocks along Forest and Cypress Avenues but at a slower rate than Bushwick's, and the industrial venue district has so far retained the zoning and noise tolerances the scene depends on.
