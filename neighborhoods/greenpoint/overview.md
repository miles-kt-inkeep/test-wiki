# Greenpoint

Greenpoint is the northernmost Brooklyn neighborhood, sitting between [Williamsburg](../williamsburg/overview.md) to the south and the Newtown Creek (the Queens border) to the north. Polish-American and working-class through most of the 20th century, it has experienced a slower, quieter version of Williamsburg's transformation. Its music presence is concentrated in two strands: the small but globally-respected metal-and-hardcore venue [Saint Vitus](./venues/saint-vitus.md), and a long roster of indie musicians who quietly relocated from Williamsburg in the 2010s.

## Geography and character

Greenpoint occupies the section of Brooklyn between the East River, Newtown Creek (the Queens border), McCarren Park and N. 14th Street (the Williamsburg border to the south), and Kingsland Avenue / Bushwick Avenue inland. The G train (Greenpoint Avenue, Nassau Avenue) connects the neighborhood north–south through Brooklyn and into Queens; the L's Bedford and Nassau stops are walkable from much of the area. Manhattan Avenue is the main commercial spine, with a cluster of music-relevant bars, record stores, and one major venue at its northern end.

The neighborhood retained its Polish-American character later than most of north Brooklyn — Polish bakeries, butchers, and the daily-attended St. Stanislaus Kostka church kept the local economy and demography distinct well into the 2010s. That late-shifting baseline gave Greenpoint a character somewhat different from the Williamsburg next door: less spectacle, more residential, slower turnover.

## Music history

### Early: working class, not a music neighborhood

Greenpoint's pre-2000 music history is largely off this wiki's frame — Polish church choirs, polka clubs along Manhattan Avenue, and a long-quiet bar scene. The neighborhood was not a destination for the rock or rap geographies of New York.

### 2000s — Williamsburg overflow

As Williamsburg rents rose, indie musicians and visual artists steadily migrated north into Greenpoint apartments. The neighborhood absorbed many of the scene's quieter actors — singer-songwriters, ambient and electronic producers, classical composers — without producing a publicly identified "Greenpoint sound." [Grizzly Bear](../../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/grizzly-bear.md) drew on a Greenpoint/Williamsburg axis through the late-2000s; Sufjan Stevens lived in the neighborhood for stretches of the 2010s; James Murphy of [LCD Soundsystem](../../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/lcd-soundsystem.md) opened the Four Horsemen wine bar at 295 Grand Street in 2015 (technically South Williamsburg, but routinely associated with the Greenpoint scene).

### 2011 onward — Saint Vitus

The neighborhood's defining institutional move was the 2011 opening of [Saint Vitus](./venues/saint-vitus.md) at 1120 Manhattan Avenue. A 250-capacity bar-and-club focused on metal, hardcore, and adjacent extreme genres, it became — within two years — globally recognized as one of the world's most important small metal venues. The list of bands that played first or formative U.S. shows at Saint Vitus from 2011 to 2024 is a who's-who of the era's underground metal: Pallbearer, Tribulation, Power Trip, Deafheaven, Sumac, Krallice, and dozens more.

### Late 2010s — institutional but small

A handful of smaller venues — Warsaw (a Polish community-center hall on Driggs Avenue that hosts indie and metal bookings), Trans-Pecos in Ridgewood (technically Queens, but Greenpoint-adjacent and routinely treated as part of the same scene) — round out the neighborhood's club ecology. A second wave of cocktail-and-listening bars (TBA, Black Rabbit) absorbs the dinner-time end of the music economy.

## Key venues

- [Saint Vitus](./venues/saint-vitus.md) — 1120 Manhattan Avenue; the metal/hardcore institution since 2011.
- Warsaw (Polish National Home) — 261 Driggs Avenue; community hall with regular concert programming and the city's most-loved post-show pierogis.
- Sunny's Bar (in Red Hook, not Greenpoint) and Pete's Candy Store (in Williamsburg) — frequently cited as part of the same north-Brooklyn small-room circuit.

## Notable artists and acts

The Greenpoint scene has had no public flagship band — its character is residential and dispersed rather than scene-shaped. The most-cited Greenpoint-resident artists in the wiki's frame are members of [Grizzly Bear](../../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/grizzly-bear.md) (Daniel Rossen and Christopher Bear at points), DIIV's Zachary Cole Smith, and Sufjan Stevens. The metal scene around Saint Vitus has functioned as the neighborhood's most globally visible musical export.

## Today

Greenpoint in 2026 has continued to gentrify but remains noticeably quieter than Williamsburg — fewer chain stores, more intact small-business stretches on Manhattan and Franklin Avenues, more functioning industrial blocks along the East River and Newtown Creek. Saint Vitus remains the neighborhood's most-watched venue. The G-train improvements of the early 2020s have made the neighborhood marginally more accessible from the rest of Brooklyn.
