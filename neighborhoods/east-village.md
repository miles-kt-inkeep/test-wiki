# East Village

The East Village runs from Houston Street north to 14th Street, and from the Bowery / Fourth Avenue east to Avenue D and the river. It is the residential other half of the [Lower East Side](./lower-east-side.md) punk story: while [CBGB](../venues/cbgb.md) sat on the Bowery's west curb (technically LES), the bands themselves ate, slept, fought, and rehearsed on St. Mark's Place, on Avenue A, and in walk-ups along East 10th Street. Through the 1980s it was also the [no wave](../genres/no-wave.md) and post-punk laboratory of the city, dense with small clubs that came and went on six-month leases.

## Geography and character

The East Village in the 1970s and 1980s was cheap, dangerous, and creatively saturated. Tompkins Square Park anchored its eastern half; St. Mark's Place between Second and Third Avenues was the commercial spine, lined with record shops, leather stores, and pizza-by-the-slice counters. Alphabet City — Avenues A through D — was the part of Manhattan that even cab drivers refused to enter at night. That isolation was the point: rents on Avenue B in 1979 ran a third of what SoHo lofts cost, and bands could afford to be bands.

## Music history

The 1970s East Village was where the [Ramones](../artists/ramones.md), [Television](../artists/television.md), and [Patti Smith](../artists/patti-smith.md) actually lived while they were turning [CBGB](../venues/cbgb.md) into a legend a few blocks south. The [New York Dolls](../artists/new-york-dolls.md) had used the Mercer Arts Center earlier in the decade, but by 1975 the locus had shifted east, and the East Village punk apartment became a kind of folkloric address.

The 1980s pulled in a different scene. The [Mudd Club](../venues/mudd-club.md), at 77 White Street, was technically over in [SoHo](./soho.md)/Tribeca, but the East Village fed it; so did [Danceteria](../venues/danceteria.md) up on West 21st in [Chelsea](./chelsea-meatpacking.md), where [Madonna](../artists/madonna.md) coat-checked before she was Madonna. East Village clubs like the Pyramid Club on Avenue A and 8BC on East 8th hosted the drag, performance-art, and [no wave](../genres/no-wave.md) overflow; [Sonic Youth](../artists/sonic-youth.md) cut their teeth in this circuit.

The 1990s brought hardcore matinees and a folk-rock revival in coffeehouses around Avenue A. By the early 2000s the East Village was again a feeder neighborhood for the Bowery Ballroom / Mercury Lounge axis, with [the Strokes](../artists/strokes.md), [LCD Soundsystem](../artists/lcd-soundsystem.md), and [Interpol](../artists/interpol.md) drinking at bars on Ludlow and Avenue A while gigging across Houston. The neighborhood produced the audience as much as the bands.

By the late 2000s, rising rents had cleared most of the working musicians out, and the cluster of tiny rock clubs — Brownies on Avenue A, Continental on St. Mark's — closed in quick succession.

## Key venues

- [CBGB](../venues/cbgb.md) — 315 Bowery; on the west edge, but the East Village was its dressing room (closed 2006).
- [Max's Kansas City](../venues/maxs-kansas-city.md) — 213 Park Avenue South; just north of the neighborhood, the Warhol-era hangout (closed 1981).
- [Mudd Club](../venues/mudd-club.md) — 77 White Street; the East Village art crowd's downtown clubhouse (closed 1983).
- [Danceteria](../venues/danceteria.md) — 30 West 21st Street; in Chelsea but populated by East Village kids (closed 1986).

## Notable artists and acts

- [Ramones](../artists/ramones.md) — Joey, Dee Dee, and Johnny lived around East 2nd Street and the Bowery.
- [Patti Smith](../artists/patti-smith.md) — lived on East 10th Street at various points.
- [Television](../artists/television.md) — Tom Verlaine and Richard Hell shared an East Village loft.
- [Sonic Youth](../artists/sonic-youth.md) — formed in East Village rehearsal spaces in 1981.
- [Madonna](../artists/madonna.md) — lived on East 4th Street in the early 1980s before her breakthrough.
- [The Strokes](../artists/strokes.md) — drank, dated, and gigged in East Village bars circa 2001.

## Today

The East Village in the 2020s is a college neighborhood with a punk costume. NYU dorms tower over former squats; St. Mark's Place is a tourist sushi-and-tattoo strip; Tompkins Square is policed and stroller-friendly. A handful of stalwarts — Niagara, Manitoba's, the Bowery Electric — keep a flame going for the rock years, but the working bands and rehearsal lofts are long gone, mostly to Brooklyn (Williamsburg, then Bushwick) and increasingly Ridgewood, Queens.
