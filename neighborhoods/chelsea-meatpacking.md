# Chelsea and the Meatpacking District

Chelsea runs roughly from 14th Street north to 30th, west of Sixth Avenue to the Hudson; the Meatpacking District is the wedge below 14th Street between Hudson Street and the river, where the wholesale meat trade ran from the 1880s into the 2000s. Together with the adjacent Hudson Square / west [SoHo](./soho.md) edge, this strip of West Side warehouse blocks inherited the late-1970s and 1980s club legacy of New York: [Paradise Garage](../venues/paradise-garage.md), Limelight, the Roxy, and [Danceteria](../venues/danceteria.md) all operated within a few avenues of each other. It is the spiritual home of New York [disco](../genres/disco.md) and the first wave of [electronic dance](../genres/electronic-dance.md) culture in America.

## Geography and character

For most of the 20th century, the area west of Eighth Avenue from Chelsea down to Houston was working warehouse and industrial space — printing plants, meat wholesalers, taxi garages. The buildings were huge, the rent was nothing, and the neighborhood emptied out at sundown, which made it ideal for clubs that wanted big rooms, all-night licenses, and no residential neighbors. Chelsea's deconsecrated churches, in particular, made spectacular venues: Limelight (47 West 20th Street) was a former Episcopal church, and the Roxy (515 West 18th) a former roller rink.

## Music history

The defining venue is [Paradise Garage](../venues/paradise-garage.md), at 84 King Street in what's now called Hudson Square — technically just below the Meatpacking District, but in the same warehouse continuum. From 1977 to 1987, with Larry Levan as resident DJ, the Garage was arguably the most influential dance club in history; "garage house" is named for it. It was members-only, mostly Black and Latino and gay, and it was where modern club culture — the long mix, the worship of the DJ, the all-night communal floor — was codified.

A few blocks north, the Roxy opened in 1978 as a roller disco and became a key hip-hop crossover venue in the early 1980s under Ruza Blue's "Wheels of Steel" nights, hosting Afrika Bambaataa and bringing [hip-hop](../genres/hip-hop.md) to a downtown audience. [Danceteria](../venues/danceteria.md) at 30 West 21st Street, opening in 1979, was the multi-floor [new wave](../genres/new-wave.md) headquarters where [Madonna](../artists/madonna.md) coat-checked, [LCD Soundsystem](../artists/lcd-soundsystem.md)'s spiritual ancestors DJed, and the [Beastie Boys](../artists/beastie-boys.md) played early shows. Limelight, opening in 1983 in the church on West 20th, ran through the Club Kid 1990s under Peter Gatien.

The Meatpacking District proper stayed industrial through the 1990s and only flipped in the early 2000s, when meatpackers were replaced by nightclubs and fashion stores; the area's musical role from then on was as nightlife real estate rather than scene. The High Line, opened in 2009 along an old freight rail above Chelsea, sealed the gentrification.

## Key venues

- [Paradise Garage](../venues/paradise-garage.md) — 84 King Street; Larry Levan's club, the most influential dance room in history (closed 1987).
- [Danceteria](../venues/danceteria.md) — 30 West 21st Street; multi-floor new-wave hub (closed 1986).
- Limelight — 47 West 20th Street; church-turned-club, Club Kid HQ (closed 2007).
- The Roxy — 515 West 18th Street; roller disco, hip-hop crossover venue (closed 2007).
- [Studio 54](../venues/studio-54.md) — 254 West 54th, technically Theater District / [Midtown](./midtown-times-square.md), but its cultural cousins lived down here.

## Notable artists and acts

- [Madonna](../artists/madonna.md) — Danceteria coat-check, Paradise Garage and Roxy regular in the early 1980s.
- [LCD Soundsystem](../artists/lcd-soundsystem.md) — James Murphy's whole project is a love letter to this club continuum.
- [Beastie Boys](../artists/beastie-boys.md) — early Danceteria shows.
- Larry Levan — Paradise Garage resident DJ; defined modern dance-music culture from this address.

## Today

The Meatpacking District is unrecognizable as warehouse country: the Whitney Museum opened at the foot of the High Line in 2015, and the streets that once held Florent and the Hellfire Club hold Hermès and Restoration Hardware. Chelsea's club row is essentially gone — Limelight closed in 2007 and was converted to a mini-mall, the Roxy in 2007. A handful of music spaces remain in the neighborhood (the Highline Ballroom operated 2007–2019, Terminal 5 sits at 56th and 11th), but the all-night warehouse-club culture that defined the area moved out — first to Williamsburg, then to Bushwick, then to Ridgewood and beyond.
