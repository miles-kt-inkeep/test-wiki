# Brooklyn — borough overview

Brooklyn is the New York borough that, since roughly 2000, has held the city's actual musical center of gravity — the rooms where the bands play, the lofts where the records get made, the projects where the rappers grew up, and the warehouses where the dance scene migrated when Manhattan's clubs were priced out. For most of the 20th century Brooklyn was a residential and industrial counterweight to Manhattan; in the 21st it has become the loud half of New York.

This page is the borough-wide narrative. The per-neighborhood pages — [Williamsburg](./williamsburg/overview.md), [Bushwick](./bushwick/overview.md), [Greenpoint](./greenpoint/overview.md), [DUMBO](./dumbo/overview.md), [Bedford-Stuyvesant](./bedford-stuyvesant/overview.md) — go deeper on the local geography, scenes, and venues.

## Geography

Brooklyn sits across the East River from Manhattan, occupying the western tip of Long Island. From north to south the music-relevant neighborhoods cluster along the river and inland from it: [Greenpoint](./greenpoint/overview.md) at the top (across the Newtown Creek from Queens), [Williamsburg](./williamsburg/overview.md) below it on the East River, [DUMBO](./dumbo/overview.md) further south under the Manhattan and Brooklyn Bridges, [Bushwick](./bushwick/overview.md) inland to the east of Williamsburg, and [Bedford-Stuyvesant](./bedford-stuyvesant/overview.md) ("Bed-Stuy") to the south of Bushwick. Subway access — the L train from Manhattan to Williamsburg/Bushwick, the G connecting the north Brooklyn neighborhoods, the A/C through Bed-Stuy — has been a precondition for nearly every Brooklyn music scene of the last forty years.

## Music history

### Pre-1990s — incubator, not destination

Brooklyn produced foundational music in this era — every borough did — but the scenes were neighborhood-specific and largely off the press radar. Bedford-Stuyvesant and the Marcy Houses in southern Williamsburg (the Marcy Projects sit just south of the Williamsburg Bridge) were producing children who would later define New York rap. The borough also hosted significant jazz activity (Cecil Taylor lived in Park Slope; the East New York and Brownsville club circuits stayed busy), reggae and dancehall in Crown Heights and East Flatbush, and a hardcore-punk scene in Park Slope and Sunset Park that fed national bands.

### 1990s — hip-hop's golden Brooklyn decade

This is when Brooklyn becomes a music capital. [The Notorious B.I.G.](../genres/hip-hop/artists/notorious-big.md) (St. James Place, Clinton Hill / Bedford-Stuyvesant border) released *Ready to Die* on Bad Boy in September 1994; [Jay-Z](../genres/hip-hop/artists/jay-z.md) (Marcy Houses, southern Williamsburg) released *Reasonable Doubt* on Roc-A-Fella in June 1996. Mos Def and Talib Kweli, both Brooklyn-based, formed Black Star in 1997. The borough's rap geography (Brownsville's M.O.P. and Sean Price, Bed-Stuy's Lil' Kim, Crown Heights' Foxy Brown) ran parallel to Manhattan's industrial machine but produced the era's most-quoted MCs.

### 2000s — the indie wave crosses the river

The defining Brooklyn-as-music-borough story is the early-2000s indie migration. The [post-punk revival](../genres/post-punk-revival/overview.md) was a Manhattan story commercially — its Mercury Lounge / Bowery Ballroom proving grounds were on the [Lower East Side](./lower-east-side/overview.md) — but its bands lived in Brooklyn from very early on, and a parallel Brooklyn-only wave was just behind. [TV on the Radio](../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/tv-on-the-radio.md), formed in Williamsburg in 2001 and recording at Stay Gold in 2003–2004, was the first widely-noticed indie band whose entire creative life ran in Brooklyn. By the mid-2000s [Grizzly Bear](../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/grizzly-bear.md), [Dirty Projectors](../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/dirty-projectors.md), Animal Collective (loosely associated), Yeasayer, and a long tail of smaller bands had made Brooklyn — and specifically Williamsburg — the address of American indie rock. The Pitchfork-era press economy and the L-train commute together made the relocation possible.

DFA Records' work and [LCD Soundsystem](../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/lcd-soundsystem.md)'s dance-punk sound were Manhattan-headquartered but Brooklyn-distributed: the warehouse parties James Murphy was DJing at and writing about in *Losing My Edge* (2002) were already happening on the wrong side of the river.

### 2010s — institutional Brooklyn

The big rooms opened. [Brooklyn Bowl](./williamsburg/venues/brooklyn-bowl.md) (2009), [Music Hall of Williamsburg](./williamsburg/venues/music-hall-of-williamsburg.md) (2007, in the old Northsix space), [Saint Vitus](./greenpoint/venues/saint-vitus.md) (2011) for metal and hardcore, and [Brooklyn Steel](./bushwick/venues/brooklyn-steel.md) (2017, the Bowery Presents 1,800-capacity East Williamsburg flagship) gave the borough a venue ladder that Manhattan no longer had. Output (Williamsburg, 2013–2018) and a long list of warehouse rooms (House of Yes, Elsewhere, Public Records, Mood Ring) carried the dance scene; the Brooklyn Mirage opened in 2017 in East Williamsburg as the city's largest open-air dance venue.

### 2019–today — Brooklyn drill

Brooklyn drill emerged out of [Bedford-Stuyvesant](./bedford-stuyvesant/overview.md), Flatbush, East Flatbush, and Canarsie around 2018–2019, drawing on the UK drill template that producers like 808Melo (London-based, frequent Brooklyn collaborator) had refined. Pop Smoke (Canarsie) released *Welcome to the Party* in April 2019 and *Meet the Woo* (mixtape) that July; he was murdered in February 2020, but his posthumous *Shoot for the Stars, Aim for the Moon* (July 2020) debuted at number one and confirmed Brooklyn drill as the borough's most commercially significant new sound in twenty years. Fivio Foreign (Canarsie), Sheff G (East Flatbush), and a crowded second wave continue the genre.

## Sound and aesthetic

There is no single "Brooklyn sound" — the borough is too big and its scenes too separate — but a few recurring tendencies hold across decades. Brooklyn music tends to be made in proximity to its audience (apartment, loft, warehouse, neighborhood club rather than industrial studio); to spike along subway lines (the L for indie, the A/C for hip-hop and drill); and to find its commercial footing later than the Manhattan equivalent (Brooklyn artists are often first noticed by the press while still self-released). The borough's racial and class geography has remained tightly mapped to its sound geography: the rap and drill scenes are anchored in Bed-Stuy / Brownsville / Canarsie, the indie scenes in Williamsburg / Bushwick / Greenpoint, and the metal and hardcore scenes around Saint Vitus and the older Park Slope / Sunset Park axis.

## Key venues

- [Music Hall of Williamsburg](./williamsburg/venues/music-hall-of-williamsburg.md) — 66 N. 6th Street, Williamsburg; the Brooklyn equivalent of [Bowery Ballroom](./lower-east-side/venues/bowery-ballroom.md), opened 2007.
- [Brooklyn Bowl](./williamsburg/venues/brooklyn-bowl.md) — 61 Wythe Avenue, Williamsburg; bowling-alley-and-concert-room hybrid that became the borough's quirkiest mid-size stage from 2009.
- [Brooklyn Steel](./bushwick/venues/brooklyn-steel.md) — 319 Frost Street, East Williamsburg; the 1,800-capacity Bowery Presents flagship that opened in 2017 and immediately became the city's most important new venue.
- [Saint Vitus](./greenpoint/venues/saint-vitus.md) — 1120 Manhattan Avenue, Greenpoint; the small metal-and-hardcore room that defined an era of underground bookings from 2011.

## Key artists with Brooklyn primary association

- [The Notorious B.I.G.](../genres/hip-hop/artists/notorious-big.md) — Bed-Stuy / Clinton Hill; the defining 1990s East Coast MC.
- [Jay-Z](../genres/hip-hop/artists/jay-z.md) — Marcy Projects, southern Williamsburg; the 1990s commercial peer to Biggie and Brooklyn's most successful rap export.
- [TV on the Radio](../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/tv-on-the-radio.md) — Williamsburg; the first widely-noticed Brooklyn-bred indie band of the 2000s.
- [Grizzly Bear](../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/grizzly-bear.md) — Williamsburg / Greenpoint; chamber-folk indie, *Veckatimest* (2009).
- [Dirty Projectors](../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/dirty-projectors.md) — Williamsburg / Bushwick; David Longstreth's polyrhythmic indie ensemble.

A great many other artists have lived or worked in Brooklyn over the decades — Mos Def, Talib Kweli, Lil' Kim, Foxy Brown, Sean Price, Mike Tyson's Bushwick years, Animal Collective's Brooklyn period, Sufjan Stevens (Carroll Gardens), Vampire Weekend's post-Columbia Brooklyn loft years, Solange, Cecil Taylor (Park Slope) — but the five above carry the per-page coverage in this wiki and are the cleanest entry points into the borough's music history.
