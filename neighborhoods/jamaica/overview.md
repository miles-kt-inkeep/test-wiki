# Jamaica

Jamaica is the Southeast Queens neighborhood that, broadly defined, contains or sits adjacent to most of the borough's rap-residential geography outside [Hollis](../hollis/overview.md) and [Long Island City](../long-island-city/overview.md). "Jamaica" here covers the Jamaica commercial center around Jamaica Avenue and 165th Street, **South Jamaica** to the south (50 Cent's neighborhood — Baisley Park and Guy Brewer Boulevard), the **St. Albans / Linden Boulevard** blocks to the east (A Tribe Called Quest's home territory), and the **Jamaica Hills / Jamaica Estates** sections to the north (where Nicki Minaj's family lived through her later childhood). The Tribe / 50 Cent / Nicki Minaj triangle is the borough's residential rap canon outside Hollis and Queensbridge.

The neighborhood does not have a unified music scene the way Hollis does — its blocks are too far apart and its musical generations too separated — but the per-artist density across Southeast Queens is high enough that Jamaica is the standard catch-all framing for "Queens hip-hop outside the famous-Hollis-block."

## Geography and character

Jamaica is the original colonial-era town center of Queens and remains the borough's most-trafficked transit hub — Jamaica Center on the E, J, and Z trains, the Long Island Rail Road's main Jamaica station, the AirTrain to JFK, and dozens of bus routes all converge here. The neighborhood is predominantly Black (with substantial Caribbean and South Asian immigrant populations) and has been since the post-WWII demographic transition. Jamaica Avenue from 160th to 168th Street is the commercial spine; Hillside Avenue carries the F train north; Sutphin Boulevard runs through the center.

**South Jamaica** (roughly south of South Road and west of Springfield Boulevard) sits around Baisley Park, the LeFrak / Baisley Park Houses, and Guy Brewer Boulevard — historically more impoverished, predominantly Black, and the residential anchor for 50 Cent and G-Unit. **St. Albans** sits east of Jamaica proper around Linden Boulevard and 197th Street — middle-class Black residential, with a long jazz-residential history (Count Basie, Lena Horne, John Coltrane, Mercer Ellington, and Ella Fitzgerald all lived in St. Albans at various points in the 1940s–1960s before the period this wiki covers).

## Music history

### 1970s–early 1980s — pre-rap Southeast Queens

In the period this wiki opens with, Southeast Queens's musical importance had already accumulated. St. Albans's "Addisleigh Park" historic district — bounded roughly by Linden Boulevard, 175th Street, 113th Avenue, and 178th Street — was the densest concentration of jazz-musician residences anywhere in New York from roughly 1940 to 1970. Count Basie's house at 174-27 Adelaide Road, Fats Waller's house at 173-19 Sayres Avenue, and Lena Horne's house at 112-45 178th Street are all on the National Register of Historic Places. By the period this wiki opens, most of those residents had passed away or relocated; the historic district remains, but the active jazz-residential scene had moved into Manhattan and Brooklyn.

### Mid-1980s — Tribe Called Quest in St. Albans

[A Tribe Called Quest](../../genres/hip-hop/artists/tribe-called-quest.md) — Q-Tip (Jonathan Davis, later Kamaal Ibn John Fareed), Phife Dawg (Malik Taylor), Ali Shaheed Muhammad, and Jarobi White — formed in 1985 while attending Murry Bergtraum High School in Manhattan, with the four members coming from a tight Southeast Queens cluster: Q-Tip and Phife were cousins-by-childhood from the Linden Boulevard / St. Albans area, Ali Shaheed Muhammad from Brooklyn-but-Tribe-anchored, Jarobi from Brooklyn-and-Queens. Q-Tip's home address at 192-15 Linden Boulevard sits one block from the wide commercial Linden Boulevard that Tribe's lyrics repeatedly name. The group's first three albums (*People's Instinctive Travels and the Paths of Rhythm*, 1990; *The Low End Theory*, 1991; *Midnight Marauders*, 1993) functionally relocated rap's alternative-canon center of gravity to the St. Albans / Linden Boulevard / Jamaica triangle.

The broader Native Tongues collective — Tribe, De La Soul (Long Island), Jungle Brothers (Manhattan / Queens), Black Sheep (Queens / North Carolina), Monie Love (London / Manhattan), Queen Latifah (Newark) — had Queens as its commercial gravitational center through the early 1990s, with Tribe as the anchor.

### 1990s–2000s — Lost Boyz, Onyx, Pharoahe Monch, 50 Cent

The Lost Boyz (Mr. Cheeks, Pretty Lou, Spigg Nice, and Freaky Tah) released *Legal Drug Money* on Universal in 1996 — *Renee* and *Music Makes Me High* turned the album platinum. Onyx — Fredro Starr, Sticky Fingaz, and Sonny Seeza — released *Bacdafucup* on Def Jam in 1993, with "Slam" hitting platinum that year; Sticky Fingaz grew up in Jamaica, Fredro Starr in Brooklyn-and-Jamaica. Pharoahe Monch (Troy Jamerson), from Jamaica, was half of Organized Konfusion through the early-90s indie rap canon and released *Internal Affairs* (Rawkus, 1999) with the platinum single "Simon Says."

The borough's commercial 2000s peak ran out of South Jamaica. Curtis "50 Cent" Jackson, raised on Guy Brewer Boulevard in South Jamaica, was signed to Columbia in 1999 but dropped after his shooting in May 2000 outside his grandmother's South Jamaica home. He resurfaced on Shady / Aftermath in 2002 (Eminem and Dr. Dre signing him jointly); *Get Rich or Die Tryin'* (February 2003) sold over 12 million copies. G-Unit — 50, Tony Yayo (Marvin Bernard), Lloyd Banks (Christopher Lloyd), and Young Buck (Tennessee) — anchored a roster that ran through *Beg for Mercy* (G-Unit, 2003) and the mid-2000s 50 Cent peak. Tony Yayo and Lloyd Banks both grew up in South Jamaica; the borough geography is tighter than the Manhattan-label commercial framing suggests.

### 2010s — Nicki Minaj and the second-wave Queens women

Onika Tanya Maraj — born in Saint James, Trinidad and Tobago, December 1982; relocated with her family to Queens in 1987 — was raised in Jamaica (the family's first US apartment was on Linden Boulevard) and Jamaica Hills, attending the LaGuardia High School for Music & Art in Manhattan. She signed to Young Money Entertainment (Lil Wayne's imprint, distributed through Universal) in 2009 and released *Pink Friday* in November 2010; *The Pinkprint* (2014) and *Queen* (2018) followed. Through the 2010–2020 decade Minaj was, by both critical and commercial measure, the most successful female rapper of the streaming era. Her Queens biography is split between Jamaica (childhood, teen years) and Long Island City (later teens) — see [Long Island City](../long-island-city/overview.md).

## Key venues

Jamaica has limited live-music venue history at the scale this wiki covers. The neighborhood's commercial-music infrastructure has been dominated by retail (record stores: Mondo Kim's-style independent shops on Jamaica Avenue through the 1990s, a long-running J&R Music outpost) and by the Apollo Theater across the East River in Harlem rather than by local clubs. **Resorts World New York City** (110-00 Rockaway Boulevard, opened 2011, expanded 2014) sits at the southern edge of South Jamaica adjacent to Aqueduct Racetrack — it is a casino, not principally a music venue, but its 7,000-capacity event center has hosted concert programming since 2020.

The neighborhood's most-photographed music site is the **South Jamaica Houses** ("the 40 projects" — actually the South Jamaica Houses #1 and #2 at 159th Street and Guy Brewer Boulevard), the public-housing complex 50 Cent's videos name-check throughout the *Get Rich or Die Tryin'* cycle.

## Notable artists and acts

- [A Tribe Called Quest](../../genres/hip-hop/artists/tribe-called-quest.md) — St. Albans / Linden Boulevard; the Native Tongues anchor.
- 50 Cent and G-Unit — South Jamaica; the commercial 2000s peak.
- Nicki Minaj — Jamaica / Jamaica Hills; the 2010s commercial peak.
- Ja Rule — Hollis-but-Jamaica-LIRR-station; the late-90s commercial peak. Primary residential coverage in the [Hollis](../hollis/overview.md) page.
- Lost Boyz — Jamaica; mid-90s platinum.
- Onyx — Jamaica / Brooklyn; the hardcore-rap commercial peak of 1993.
- Pharoahe Monch — Jamaica; the lyrical-rap canon of the late 1990s and early 2000s.
- Tragedy Khadafi (Intelligent Hoodlum) — Jamaica / Queensbridge cross-residential.

## Today

Jamaica in 2026 remains a major transit hub and the borough's most diverse single neighborhood. Real-estate pressure has touched Jamaica Center most heavily (a wave of high-rise development around the AirTrain since 2018) but South Jamaica's residential blocks remain largely intact. No major music venue or museum anchors the neighborhood's commercial-music history; the rap-historic sites are mostly private addresses with no public marker.
