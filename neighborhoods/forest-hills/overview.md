# Forest Hills

Forest Hills is the Central Queens neighborhood whose music history sits at two distant but equally legible points: the **Ramones**' origin (the four founding members all attended Forest Hills High School in the late 1960s and formed the band in early 1974) and **Forest Hills Stadium** (the West Side Tennis Club's 13,000-capacity former U.S. Open tennis stadium, renovated for concerts in 2013 and now the city's primary outdoor seasonal concert venue). The neighborhood between those points has never been a residential scene the way [Hollis](../hollis/overview.md) or [Queensbridge](../long-island-city/overview.md) have been — Forest Hills's music story is bookended by the two institutions, not built between them.

## Geography and character

Forest Hills sits in central Queens, bounded roughly by Long Island Expressway to the north, Jewel Avenue to the south, Yellowstone Boulevard to the east, and the Forest Park Golf Course to the west. The E, F, M, and R trains all stop at the 71st Avenue / Forest Hills station; the Long Island Rail Road has a station on the Main Line. The neighborhood is high-density apartment buildings around Queens Boulevard with a quieter section to the south — **Forest Hills Gardens** — a private community of stone-clad Tudor and Georgian houses designed by Frederick Law Olmsted Jr. and Grosvenor Atterbury in 1909. The West Side Tennis Club, which hosted the U.S. Open tennis championships from 1915 to 1977 (with a few wartime gaps), sits at the southern edge of Forest Hills Gardens. The neighborhood is predominantly white and middle-class through the period this wiki covers, with significant Jewish (especially Bukharian after 1990) and East Asian populations.

## Music history

### Forest Hills High School and the Ramones (1968–1974)

Forest Hills High School is at 67-01 110th Street, two blocks south of the F train. Its 1968 to 1972 graduating classes contained, in different years, John Cummings (later Johnny Ramone), Douglas Colvin (later Dee Dee Ramone), Tommy Erdelyi (later Tommy Ramone, born in Budapest, immigrated to Forest Hills as a child), and Jeffrey Hyman (later Joey Ramone, who lived in Forest Hills with his mother after his parents' divorce). The four met locally and began rehearsing as a band — originally as a glam-rock outfit playing Stooges covers — in early 1974. By that summer they had taken the name "Ramones" (after Paul McCartney's Silver Beatles-era pseudonym Paul Ramon), worked out the leather-jackets-and-jeans uniform, and begun playing CBGB. The Ramones' Manhattan story is covered in detail on the [Ramones page](../../genres/punk-rock/artists/ramones.md) and the [punk rock overview](../../genres/punk-rock/overview.md) — but their Queens origin is foundational. The leather jacket and bowl-cut were as much Queens-suburban-kid as they were Lower-East-Side-rock-and-roll; the Ramones imported a working-class Queens aesthetic into a Manhattan scene that, until them, was leaning aristocratic and art-school.

Joey Ramone died in April 2001; Forest Hills's 67th Avenue at Yellowstone Boulevard was renamed "Joey Ramone Place" — actually, the renaming was on the Lower East Side at Bowery and 2nd Street near CBGB, but the *Forest Hills* equivalent is the unofficial Forest Hills High School Ramones memorial mural in the gymnasium hallway and the band-history tour that runs out of the high school's alumni association.

### Mid-century Forest Hills and Simon & Garfunkel

Paul Simon and Art Garfunkel both attended Forest Hills High School and graduated in 1958 — six years before the Ramones cohort started showing up. The two recorded as Tom & Jerry from 1957 before becoming Simon & Garfunkel by the early 1960s. Their formative music biography is pre-this-wiki's-coverage-period and Manhattan-relocated by the time of *Sounds of Silence* (1965), but the Forest Hills High School class of 1958 is worth flagging — between Simon, Garfunkel, and the Ramones cohort a decade later, the same building had a hand in two of the most influential American bands of the postwar era.

### 1977 — The U.S. Open's last Forest Hills year, the stadium dormant

The U.S. Open relocated from the West Side Tennis Club to Flushing Meadows–Corona Park in 1978; through the 1980s and 1990s the Forest Hills Stadium hosted occasional concerts (Frank Sinatra played in 1980, Bob Dylan in 1981, Diana Ross several times) but its main use shrank to club tennis and corporate events. By 2005 the stadium was effectively dormant, with the structure deteriorating and the surrounding Forest Hills Gardens community ambivalent about its concert future.

### 2013–today — Forest Hills Stadium as concert venue

In 2013 the Madison House Presents promoter group (in partnership with the West Side Tennis Club) reopened [Forest Hills Stadium](./venues/forest-hills-stadium.md) for a renovated concert season. The opening 2013 lineup included Mumford & Sons (the soft-launch test event) and Paul Simon (the inaugural full concert — a return to the place he had been a high-school student). The stadium has since hosted a 25-to-35-date summer season every year (skipping 2020 for COVID), with headliners ranging from Vampire Weekend, the Strokes, and LCD Soundsystem to Jack White, Mumford & Sons, the Black Keys, Hozier, the Killers, Belle & Sebastian, Solange, Phoebe Bridgers, and Olivia Rodrigo. The 13,000 capacity makes it the largest outdoor concert venue inside city limits.

Tensions between the venue and the Forest Hills Gardens private community over noise, traffic, and parking have surfaced periodically; the operating agreement is renegotiated periodically but the venue has continued through every season since 2013.

## Key venues

- [Forest Hills Stadium](./venues/forest-hills-stadium.md) — 1 Tennis Place, Forest Hills; the West Side Tennis Club's 13,000-capacity former U.S. Open stadium, renovated for concerts in 2013, the city's primary outdoor seasonal concert room.

Outside the stadium, Forest Hills's music venue geography is light — a few pre-2010 club spaces on Queens Boulevard, a handful of bars with occasional live programming, but nothing programmed nationally. The Russo's on the Bay event space (technically Howard Beach but Forest Hills-adjacent in the bookings community) hosts occasional rap and Latin concerts.

## Notable artists and acts

- The [Ramones](../../genres/punk-rock/artists/ramones.md) — Forest Hills High School, 1968–1972; the band's Queens-suburban-kid origin is foundational to punk's broader Manhattan story.
- Paul Simon and Art Garfunkel — Forest Hills High School, class of 1958; pre-this-wiki's-coverage-period but the cohort is worth noting.
- Action Bronson (Ariyan Arslani) — born and raised in Forest Hills; his early-2010s catalog (*Dr. Lecter*, 2011; *Blue Chips*, 2012) carries the neighborhood biographical detail.
- Cyndi Lauper — born and raised in Ozone Park / South Queens, not Forest Hills, but lived in Forest Hills for several years pre-fame and is sometimes claimed by the neighborhood.

## Today

Forest Hills in 2026 remains a high-density, high-income central Queens neighborhood; the stadium continues to be the borough's principal concert venue; the high school continues its post-Ramones reputation as an unintentional rock-history landmark. The neighborhood is a popular subject of New York-specific real-estate and music journalism (the *New York Times* and *Curbed* run periodic Forest Hills Stadium concert-season previews) but is not, in 2026, a residential-musician scene.
