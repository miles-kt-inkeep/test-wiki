# Forest Hills Stadium

Forest Hills Stadium is a 13,000-capacity outdoor concert venue at 1 Tennis Place, [Forest Hills](../overview.md), Queens, owned and operated by the **West Side Tennis Club**. The stadium was built in 1923 as the host of the U.S. National Championships (later the U.S. Open) tennis tournament, hosted the U.S. Open through 1977, sat largely dormant through the 1980s, 1990s, and 2000s, and was renovated and reopened as a permanent summer concert venue in 2013. It is now the largest outdoor concert venue inside New York City limits and the only one with a continuously-booked annual summer season.

## History

### 1923–1977 — the U.S. Open era

The West Side Tennis Club (founded 1892 in Manhattan, relocated to Forest Hills in 1913) opened the 14,000-capacity Forest Hills Stadium in August 1923 as the host of that year's U.S. National Championships. From 1923 through 1977 the stadium hosted the U.S. Open (then the U.S. National Championships, renamed the U.S. Open in 1968), with brief wartime gaps. The stadium hosted occasional concerts through the 1960s — the Beatles played August 28, 1964, as part of their first North American tour; Bob Dylan played his "Royal Albert Hall"-style folk-rock-electric set August 28, 1965; Frank Sinatra, the Who, Diana Ross, Barbra Streisand, Jimi Hendrix, and Led Zeppelin all played dates in the late 1960s and early 1970s. The U.S. Open relocated to the new National Tennis Center in Flushing Meadows–Corona Park in 1978.

### 1978–2012 — dormancy and decay

Through the 1980s, 1990s, and 2000s the stadium hosted occasional concerts but its main use shrank to club tennis events and corporate functions. The structure deteriorated significantly — the wooden seating was weather-damaged, the field surface degraded, and the surrounding Forest Hills Gardens private community grew ambivalent about hosting large events. By 2010 the stadium was effectively dormant; the West Side Tennis Club explored selling or partially demolishing it. The club's membership voted against demolition in 2010 and 2011; in 2012 the club approved a partnership with Madison House Presents (the Colorado-based concert promoter) for a renovated concert season.

### 2013–present — Madison House Presents renovation and revival

Madison House Presents (later joined and replaced by other promoters in stages — Tiësto's promoter, then the New York-based Mike Luba team) led a partial renovation in 2013: new modular seating, new stage and PA, refurbished concourses, and a redesigned operating-and-booking agreement with the Forest Hills Gardens community. The opening 2013 season included a small Mumford & Sons soft-launch test event in August and a full Paul Simon concert in September (a Forest Hills High School class-of-1958 alumnus returning to his hometown's signature venue). The Strokes, Vampire Weekend, and Mumford & Sons headlined dates the following summer.

From 2014 onward Forest Hills Stadium has hosted 25-to-35 dates per summer (with the 2020 season canceled and the 2021 season truncated due to COVID). The headliner roster has been a tour stop for nearly every act of relevant scale: Vampire Weekend (multiple dates), the Strokes, LCD Soundsystem (with three nights in August 2018 as part of their reunion-tour run), Jack White, the Black Keys, Hozier, Mumford & Sons (every year through 2019), Belle & Sebastian, Solange, Phoebe Bridgers, Olivia Rodrigo, Maggie Rogers, the National, Father John Misty, Bon Iver, Tame Impala, Khruangbin, Tyler Childers, Patti Smith, and a long roster of indie, classic-rock, jam-band, and crossover-pop acts.

### Operating model and Forest Hills Gardens

The stadium operates under a renewable agreement with the West Side Tennis Club and the Forest Hills Gardens Corporation (the private residential association that owns the surrounding streets). Sound mitigation (most concerts curfew at 10:30 PM, occasionally extended), parking and traffic agreements (concertgoers are routed away from the residential streets), and a ticketed-only-but-public-access policy form the spine of the relationship. The agreement has been renegotiated periodically; tensions surface every few years over noise, parking, and the broader question of whether 13,000-person concerts belong inside a private residential community. The stadium has continued operating through every dispute.

## Building and layout

The stadium is a horseshoe-shaped concrete-and-steel structure with the open end facing southeast. Capacity is 13,000 for concert configurations (lower for tennis), with bleacher-style seating on three sides and a temporary modular stage at the open end. The acoustics are unusually good for an outdoor venue of this size — the closed sides reflect sound back toward the open end without creating the boomy reverb that most outdoor amphitheaters suffer. The structure is on the National Register of Historic Places and is the city's last surviving early-20th-century outdoor stadium of its kind (Ebbets Field, the Polo Grounds, and Shea Stadium are all gone).

The venue's official address is 1 Tennis Place, an internal access road inside Forest Hills Gardens. Walk-in access from the 67th Avenue / Forest Hills Stadium subway stop (10-minute walk) and the Forest Hills LIRR station (5-minute walk) is the primary concertgoer route.

## Place in the wiki

Forest Hills Stadium is, in a Queens-borough frame, the equivalent of the [Bowery Ballroom](../../lower-east-side/venues/bowery-ballroom.md) and [Brooklyn Steel](../../bushwick/venues/brooklyn-steel.md) — a flagship room at the borough's institutional concert peak — but at a much larger capacity than either. In a citywide frame it functions alongside Barclays Center, Madison Square Garden, and the SummerStage outdoor series as one of the city's primary concert venues; its 13,000-capacity, outdoor, season-bounded role is distinct from any of those. The venue is the borough's most important post-2013 music-historic site.
