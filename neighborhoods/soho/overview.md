# SoHo

SoHo — South of Houston — is bounded by Houston Street to the north, Canal Street to the south, Lafayette to the east, and West Broadway / Sixth Avenue to the west. Through the 1970s it was a half-emptied manufacturing district whose cast-iron loft buildings, illegal to live in until the 1971 "loft law," became cheap shelter for artists and musicians. SoHo was less a venue neighborhood than a residential and rehearsal one: the [no wave](../../genres/no-wave/overview.md) scene, loft jazz, and the early downtown art-music crossover all happened in private spaces here. The [Mudd Club](venues/mudd-club.md), just south on White Street in what is technically Tribeca, served as the scene's public face.

## Geography and character

In the early 1970s, SoHo's cast-iron loft buildings sat half-vacant, recently abandoned by the textile and printing trades. Artists moved in for pennies a square foot, often illegally, and the city's 1971 loft law eventually formalized the arrangement. The combination of huge raw spaces and zero residential infrastructure produced a particular kind of culture: rehearsal rooms, gallery openings, and one-off performance nights inside private lofts, rather than a street of clubs. Mercer Street, Wooster Street, and Greene Street were the densest of these blocks; the Kitchen, an experimental performance space founded in 1971, anchored the avant-garde end.

## Music history

In the mid-to-late 1970s, SoHo's lofts hosted the loft jazz movement — players like Sam Rivers, Ornette Coleman, and David Murray running their own venues out of their living spaces because the [Greenwich Village](../greenwich-village/overview.md) jazz clubs had closed ranks. Rivers's Studio Rivbea on Bond Street (just east of SoHo proper) is the canonical example. The same buildings supported the minimalist composers — Philip Glass and Steve Reich both rehearsed and lived in SoHo lofts — and the visual artists who would soon collide with the rock world.

That collision was [no wave](../../genres/no-wave/overview.md). Beginning around 1978, [Sonic Youth](../../genres/no-wave/artists/sonic-youth.md), Lydia Lunch, James Chance, DNA, and the Theoretical Girls played short, scraped, atonal sets in SoHo lofts and at the [Mudd Club](venues/mudd-club.md) at 77 White Street. The Mudd Club, opened by Steve Mass in 1978, was the after-hours alternative to [Studio 54](../midtown-times-square/venues/studio-54.md): downtown, art-friendly, and a scene shared with the [East Village](../east-village/overview.md) and Tribeca. [Madonna](../../genres/electronic-dance/artists/madonna.md) went to the Mudd Club; so did Basquiat, Debbie Harry of [Blondie](../../genres/punk-rock/artists/blondie.md), and most of the [punk](../../genres/punk-rock/overview.md) scene on a night off.

By the mid-1980s, SoHo's gallery boom and the loft law's eventual gentrification effects had pushed musicians out. The [Knitting Factory](venues/knitting-factory.md), an experimental venue founded in 1987, started on East Houston and migrated through several SoHo-adjacent addresses, carrying the avant-garde flag through the 1990s before eventually moving to Brooklyn.

## Key venues

- [Mudd Club](venues/mudd-club.md) — 77 White Street (Tribeca line); the downtown answer to Studio 54 (closed 1983).
- [Knitting Factory](venues/knitting-factory.md) — multiple SoHo-adjacent addresses; downtown experimental hub before its Brooklyn move.
- The Kitchen — 484 Broome Street originally; experimental performance space, still operating in Chelsea.
- Studio Rivbea — 24 Bond Street; Sam Rivers's loft, defining venue of the loft jazz era (closed late 1970s).

## Notable artists and acts

- [Sonic Youth](../../genres/no-wave/artists/sonic-youth.md) — formed and rehearsed in SoHo lofts in the early 1980s.
- [Madonna](../../genres/electronic-dance/artists/madonna.md) — Mudd Club regular before her 1983 breakthrough.
- [Blondie](../../genres/punk-rock/artists/blondie.md) — crossover figures between the SoHo art scene and the CBGB rock scene.
- [Beastie Boys](../../genres/hip-hop/artists/beastie-boys.md) — formed as a hardcore band in 1981 with deep SoHo art-scene ties.

## Today

SoHo is now one of the most expensive shopping districts on earth. The cast-iron buildings hold flagship boutiques, and the lofts above them are condo conversions starting in the seven figures. The art world largely decamped to Chelsea, then partly to the Lower East Side gallery scene; the experimental music world followed the [Knitting Factory](venues/knitting-factory.md) to Brooklyn. SoHo's musical residue is mostly architectural — the buildings are still beautiful, the music isn't here anymore.
