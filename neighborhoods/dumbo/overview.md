# DUMBO

DUMBO ("Down Under the Manhattan Bridge Overpass") is the small Brooklyn neighborhood occupying a few cobblestone-paved blocks under the Manhattan and Brooklyn Bridges, on the East River across from the [Lower East Side](../lower-east-side/overview.md) and Financial District. Its music story is shorter than the other Brooklyn neighborhoods covered in this wiki, but it has produced one important venue — the now-closed Galapagos Art Space — and remains a small institutional anchor for performance art, classical music, and the quieter end of the indie scene.

## Geography and character

DUMBO sits between Brooklyn Bridge Park to the north, Vinegar Hill to the east, the Brooklyn Heights bluff to the south, and the East River to the west. Its cobblestone streets and surviving 19th-century industrial buildings make it visually distinctive — there is no other Brooklyn neighborhood that looks like this — but the neighborhood is also small (roughly 12 blocks at its widest), and gentrification since the early 2000s has pushed most of the original artist population out. By 2026 DUMBO is a luxury-residential and commercial-tech district with a thin layer of cultural programming. The F and A/C trains stop at York and High Streets; the East River Ferry runs to Manhattan.

## Music history

### 1995–2008 — Galapagos Art Space

DUMBO's defining music venue was Galapagos Art Space, opened in 1995 at 70 N. 6th Street in [Williamsburg](../williamsburg/overview.md) by Robert Elmes and Philippa Kaye, before relocating to DUMBO at 16 Main Street in 2008. The space was a hybrid bar / cabaret / experimental-music room, hosting downtown jazz, performance art, indie rock, burlesque, and the long-running Floating Kabarette series. Both locations were instrumental to the early-to-mid-2000s Brooklyn experimental scene; the DUMBO incarnation closed in 2014 when Elmes relocated the project to Detroit.

### 2010s — Brooklyn Academy of Music adjacency

DUMBO's residential character has kept large concert venues out, but the neighborhood benefits from proximity to Brooklyn Academy of Music (BAM, in Fort Greene, walkable from DUMBO) and to St. Ann's Warehouse, which moved from Brooklyn Heights to a converted Tobacco Warehouse on Water Street in DUMBO in 2015. St. Ann's programs theater primarily but includes regular concert and music-theater bookings.

### 2010s onward — institutional

DUMBO's other consistent music presence is BRIC, the multi-arts nonprofit (technically headquartered in Fort Greene at the Fulton Street BRIC House but with overlapping DUMBO programming) whose Celebrate Brooklyn! summer concert series in Prospect Park is the borough's most-attended free music event. There is no DUMBO-specific concert venue at scale.

## Key venues

- St. Ann's Warehouse (2015– ) — 45 Water Street; theater-and-music venue in the converted Tobacco Warehouse.
- Galapagos Art Space (DUMBO incarnation, 2008–2014) — 16 Main Street; experimental performance and music space.
- The Brooklyn Bridge Park amphitheater hosts occasional summer concert programming but is not a regular venue.

## Notable artists and acts

DUMBO has not been a band-producing neighborhood; its music presence is venue- and series-shaped. The most-cited DUMBO-resident artists tend to be in adjacent disciplines (visual art, dance, theater) rather than rock or hip-hop.

## Today

DUMBO in 2026 is one of the most-photographed neighborhoods in Brooklyn — the Manhattan Bridge framing of Washington Street is on more Instagram feeds than any music venue could rival — but its scale-and-rent profile keeps it institutional rather than scene-shaped. The neighborhood functions as an extension of the larger Brooklyn music ecosystem (BAM, BRIC, Brooklyn Heights) rather than as its own scene.
