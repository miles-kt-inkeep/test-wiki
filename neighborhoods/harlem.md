# Harlem

Harlem occupies upper Manhattan from roughly 110th Street to 155th, between the Hudson and Harlem Rivers, with Central Harlem — its musical heart — running from Fifth Avenue west to St. Nicholas. It is the single most important neighborhood in the history of Black American music: the [jazz](../genres/jazz.md) capital of the world from the 1920s through the 1950s, the home stage of the [Apollo Theater](../venues/apollo-theater.md), and, beginning in the 1990s, a steadily growing presence in [hip-hop](../genres/hip-hop.md) through Mase, Cam'ron and Dipset, and later [A$AP Mob](../artists/asap-mob.md).

## Geography and character

125th Street is Harlem's main commercial artery and the address of the [Apollo Theater](../venues/apollo-theater.md), at 253 West 125th. Lenox Avenue (now Malcolm X Boulevard) and Seventh Avenue (Adam Clayton Powell Jr. Boulevard) are the north-south spines; the brownstone blocks of Strivers' Row on West 138th and 139th Streets house some of the city's most beautiful late-19th-century housing stock. To the west, Sugar Hill — roughly 145th to 155th between Edgecombe and Amsterdam — was where successful Harlem Renaissance artists lived, and where Duke Ellington kept an apartment. The geography matters because Harlem's music has always been neighborhood music: clubs, churches, and theaters within walking distance of each other.

## Music history

The Harlem Renaissance of the 1920s made the neighborhood the global center of [jazz](../genres/jazz.md). The Cotton Club (originally at 142nd and Lenox) hosted Duke Ellington and Cab Calloway through the late 1920s and early 1930s; the Savoy Ballroom on Lenox between 140th and 141st was the integrated dance hall where Lindy Hop was born; and Connie's Inn, the Lafayette, and the Apollo competed for the same audience. By the early 1940s, Minton's Playhouse at 210 West 118th was hosting after-hours jam sessions where Charlie Parker, Dizzy Gillespie, and Thelonious Monk worked out the language of bebop.

The [Apollo Theater](../venues/apollo-theater.md), opening to Black audiences in 1934, became the proving ground for Black popular music across genres: Ella Fitzgerald won its first Amateur Night in 1934; Sarah Vaughan, James Brown, Aretha Franklin, the Jackson 5, and later Lauryn Hill all played defining shows there.

The 1970s and 1980s were a fallow period commercially — Harlem suffered with the rest of the city — but the cultural foundation held. When [hip-hop](../genres/hip-hop.md) broke uptown in the late 1970s and 1980s, Harlem was a natural feeder: Kurtis Blow grew up here, and Big L emerged in the early 1990s. By the late 1990s, Cam'ron, Mase, and the Dipset crew had made Harlem a recognized hip-hop neighborhood in its own right, distinct from Brooklyn or Queens. In the 2010s, [A$AP Mob](../artists/asap-mob.md) — A$AP Rocky, A$AP Ferg, and the rest — pulled the Harlem hip-hop tradition into the streetwear and internet eras, recording and shooting videos throughout the neighborhood.

## Key venues

- [Apollo Theater](../venues/apollo-theater.md) — 253 West 125th Street; the most important Black music venue in America, still operating.
- Minton's Playhouse — 206 West 118th Street; birthplace of bebop, reopened as Minton's after several closures.
- Cotton Club — original location at 644 Lenox; Ellington-era flagship (closed 1940 at Harlem location).
- Savoy Ballroom — 596 Lenox; integrated dance hall, home of the Lindy Hop (closed 1958, demolished).

## Notable artists and acts

- [A$AP Mob](../artists/asap-mob.md) — Harlem-formed collective, the neighborhood's defining 2010s act.
- Cam'ron / Dipset — late-1990s and 2000s Harlem hip-hop standard-bearers.
- Duke Ellington — Cotton Club resident bandleader; Sugar Hill apartment dweller.
- Billie Holiday — Apollo and uptown club regular through the late 1930s.
- James Brown — recorded the legendary *Live at the Apollo* in 1962.

## Today

Harlem in the 2020s is gentrifying steadily — 125th Street has chains, Whole Foods opened in 2017 — but it remains, demographically and culturally, a Black neighborhood with a working music scene. The [Apollo Theater](../venues/apollo-theater.md) is a National Historic Landmark and a busy venue. Minton's has reopened as a jazz supper club. The hip-hop scene is more diffuse, with A$AP-affiliated artists scattered across Manhattan, Brooklyn, and Los Angeles, but Harlem still produces rappers — and Harlem still claims them.
