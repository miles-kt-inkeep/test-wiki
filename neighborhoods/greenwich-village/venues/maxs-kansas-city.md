# Max's Kansas City

Max's Kansas City was a restaurant and nightclub at 213 Park Avenue South, just north of Union Square in [Greenwich Village](../overview.md)'s northern edge, that operated from December 1965 to 1981. Founded by Mickey Ruskin, it functioned as a salon for the downtown art world before evolving into a primary live-music venue for the [New York Dolls](../../../genres/punk-rock/artists/new-york-dolls.md), [Blondie](../../../genres/punk-rock/artists/blondie.md), and the early [punk rock](../../../genres/punk-rock/overview.md) scene. The back room — bathed in Dan Flavin's red fluorescent sculpture — was Andy Warhol's nightly headquarters.

## Location and history

Mickey Ruskin opened Max's on December 6, 1965, naming it after a friend's joke about a steakhouse in Kansas City. The ground floor served chickpeas and steak to artists who paid in artwork; the back room became Warhol's salon, drawing Lou Reed, Nico, and the entire Factory orbit. A second-floor cabaret room was added in 1969 and began hosting live music in earnest by 1970.

Ruskin lost the lease in 1974, and the club was reopened later that year by Tommy Dean, who shifted programming squarely toward rock and the emerging punk scene. Max's closed for good in 1981, the same year a wave of Bowery clubs were taking its audience.

## Scene and significance

The Velvet Underground's final shows with Lou Reed were recorded upstairs in August 1970 and released as *Live at Max's Kansas City*. Through the early 1970s, Max's competed with [CBGB](../../lower-east-side/venues/cbgb.md) for the same downtown bands; many played both rooms in the same week. The [New York Dolls](../../../genres/punk-rock/artists/new-york-dolls.md) effectively launched glam-punk from its upstairs stage, and [Blondie](../../../genres/punk-rock/artists/blondie.md), [Patti Smith](../../../genres/punk-rock/artists/patti-smith.md), and the [Ramones](../../../genres/punk-rock/artists/ramones.md) all built early followings there.

Beyond rock, Max's hosted Bob Marley's first New York shows opening for Bruce Springsteen in 1973, plus [no wave](../../../genres/no-wave/overview.md) and proto-[new wave](../../../genres/new-wave/overview.md) bills that defined the late-1970s downtown sound.

## Notable performances / residencies

- The Velvet Underground — summer 1970 residency, the source of the famous live LP.
- [New York Dolls](../../../genres/punk-rock/artists/new-york-dolls.md) — anchor band for the upstairs room, 1972–1974.
- Bob Marley & the Wailers — opening for Bruce Springsteen, July 1973.
- [Blondie](../../../genres/punk-rock/artists/blondie.md) — early sets with Television Personalities and others, 1975.
- Sid Vicious — solo shows in September 1978 that produced his only solo live album.
- [Ramones](../../../genres/punk-rock/artists/ramones.md) — repeated bookings during the CBGB cross-over era.
- [Patti Smith](../../../genres/punk-rock/artists/patti-smith.md) — readings and early band shows.

## Today

Max's Kansas City closed in 1981. The building at 213 Park Avenue South has housed a deli, a Korean restaurant, and most recently retail space. It was not landmarked, and a small commemorative plaque is the only marker. The Max's Kansas City Project, founded by Yvonne Sewall-Ruskin, continues to administer a grant fund for working artists in Mickey Ruskin's name.
