# Williamsburg

Williamsburg is the North Brooklyn neighborhood that, between roughly 2000 and 2015, became the address of American indie rock and the most-watched gentrification story in the city. Bounded by the East River to the west, McCarren Park / [Greenpoint](../greenpoint/overview.md) to the north, [Bushwick](../bushwick/overview.md) and East Williamsburg to the east, and the Williamsburg Bridge approach / Marcy Houses to the south, it is also the borough's most direct cultural competitor to the Manhattan neighborhoods — [Lower East Side](../lower-east-side/overview.md) and [East Village](../east-village/overview.md) — that the L train connects it to in two stops.

## Geography and character

The neighborhood divides into a few rough zones. North Williamsburg (north of Grand Street) is the post-2000 rebuild — converted warehouses, mid-rise condos, the Bedford Avenue commercial strip — and is where most of the wiki-relevant venues sit. South Williamsburg, dominated by the Hasidic Jewish community along Bedford and Roebling, is largely outside the music story. East Williamsburg (east of Union Avenue) blurs into Bushwick and contains the warehouse-show district and [Brooklyn Steel](../bushwick/venues/brooklyn-steel.md). The Marcy Houses (between the Williamsburg Bridge and Flushing Avenue) are technically in southern Williamsburg but cultural geography ties them to [Bedford-Stuyvesant](../bedford-stuyvesant/overview.md); they are where [Jay-Z](../../genres/hip-hop/artists/jay-z.md) grew up.

What made the neighborhood music-relevant in the late 1990s was a particular combination: cheap industrial loft space (the waterfront was still mostly working warehouses), a 12-minute L-train commute to the [Lower East Side](../lower-east-side/overview.md), and a bohemian-identifying population that had been priced out of the East Village. By 2005 those conditions had vanished, but the inertia of the scene — the bands, the labels, the press, the rooms — kept Williamsburg the indie-rock address for another decade.

## Music history

### Early-2000s loft and DIY years

Before the official rooms opened, the scene was loft-based: bands rehearsed in the warehouse spaces along Kent Avenue, North 6th, and Wythe, threw parties in the same buildings, and self-promoted via post-its and early Myspace. Northsix opened in 2001 at 66 N. 6th Street as the neighborhood's first dedicated indie-rock club; that building was rebuilt in 2007 as [Music Hall of Williamsburg](./venues/music-hall-of-williamsburg.md) under Bowery Presents and remains the neighborhood's flagship room. The most important Brooklyn-bred band of this period was [TV on the Radio](../../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/tv-on-the-radio.md), whose Stay Gold studio sessions in 2003–2004 produced *Desperate Youth, Blood Thirsty Babes* — the first widely-noticed indie record made entirely in Brooklyn rather than Manhattan.

### Mid-2000s indie boom

By the mid-2000s every active U.S. indie band either lived in Williamsburg or stayed there when in town. [Grizzly Bear](../../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/grizzly-bear.md), [Dirty Projectors](../../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/dirty-projectors.md), Yeasayer, the Walkmen, and dozens of smaller bands worked out of converted lofts. McCarren Park Pool — emptied in 1984 and unrebuilt — became the neighborhood's de-facto outdoor venue from 2006 to 2008, hosting free Pool Parties that drew tens of thousands. Pitchfork's growing influence as a press economy was deeply tied to this geography.

### Late 2000s onward — institutional Williamsburg

Music Hall of Williamsburg opened in 2007. [Brooklyn Bowl](./venues/brooklyn-bowl.md) opened at 61 Wythe Avenue in 2009 — a 600-capacity bowling-alley-and-concert hybrid that booked everything from indie rock to funk to ?uestlove residencies. Output (74 Wythe Avenue, 2013–2018) brought a Berlin-style techno room to the neighborhood. By the mid-2010s the L-train shutdown threat (eventually downgraded) and the relentless rent rise had pushed most of the actual bands deeper east into Bushwick and Ridgewood, but the venues remained.

## Key venues

- [Music Hall of Williamsburg](./venues/music-hall-of-williamsburg.md) — 66 N. 6th Street; the neighborhood's flagship indie-rock room since 2007.
- [Brooklyn Bowl](./venues/brooklyn-bowl.md) — 61 Wythe Avenue; the bowling-alley concert hybrid that became Williamsburg's quirkiest mid-size stage.
- Northsix (1999–2007) — the predecessor at 66 N. 6th Street; rebuilt as Music Hall of Williamsburg.
- McCarren Park Pool (2006–2008) — outdoor concert venue in the empty pool basin; hosted the era's defining free Pool Parties.
- Output (2013–2018) — 74 Wythe Avenue; the Berlin-influenced techno club that defined Williamsburg dance music in the 2010s.

## Notable artists and acts

- [TV on the Radio](../../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/tv-on-the-radio.md) — formed 2001 in a Williamsburg loft.
- [Grizzly Bear](../../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/grizzly-bear.md) — Williamsburg / Greenpoint, mid-2000s onward.
- [Dirty Projectors](../../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/dirty-projectors.md) — Williamsburg / Bushwick.
- [Jay-Z](../../genres/hip-hop/artists/jay-z.md) — grew up in the Marcy Houses in southern Williamsburg, though Bed-Stuy / Brooklyn-at-large carry the cultural identification.
- [LCD Soundsystem](../../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/lcd-soundsystem.md) — Manhattan-headquartered, but James Murphy and the DFA crew were Williamsburg fixtures throughout the 2000s.

## Today

Williamsburg in 2026 is unrecognizable from its 2003 self. Bedford Avenue is mostly chain stores; the Domino Sugar Refinery has been redeveloped; the waterfront is luxury towers. The L train still runs. Music Hall of Williamsburg, Brooklyn Bowl, and a smaller circuit of bars (Baby's All Right on the Williamsburg/South-Williamsburg border, opened 2013) keep the music geography alive, but the cheap-loft ecosystem that made the original scene possible is gone. The actual scenes have moved east to [Bushwick](../bushwick/overview.md) and beyond.
