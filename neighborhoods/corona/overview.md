# Corona

Corona is the central Queens neighborhood that sits at an unusual intersection in this wiki's frame: it contains the borough's most-visited music-historic site (the **Louis Armstrong House Museum** at 34-56 107th Street, where Armstrong lived from 1943 until his death in July 1971), it produced one of the canonical late-1980s Juice Crew MCs (**Kool G Rap**), and it has otherwise stayed off the post-1970s music-scene map. The Armstrong residency is foundational for jazz biography but barely overlaps with the wiki's coverage period; the Kool G Rap connection is the cleanest active-era residential music story Corona offers. Between the two, the neighborhood is at the edge of the wiki's frame — included here because Armstrong's house is the borough's most-cited single music site, not because the neighborhood produced a 1980s-onward scene.

## Geography and character

Corona sits in central Queens between Elmhurst to the west, Flushing to the east, Jackson Heights to the north, and Forest Hills / Rego Park to the south. The 7 train runs along Roosevelt Avenue with stops at 103rd Street, 111th Street, and Junction Boulevard. The neighborhood is overwhelmingly Latino (predominantly Dominican and Mexican; significant Ecuadorian, Colombian, and Salvadoran populations) and has been since the late 1970s — Corona was one of the first Queens neighborhoods to undergo the postwar Latino-immigrant demographic transition. Its main commercial strips are Roosevelt Avenue (the elevated 7-train spine) and 108th Street.

The **Louis Armstrong House** is at 34-56 107th Street, two blocks north of Roosevelt Avenue. The new **Louis Armstrong Center**, a 75,000-square-foot programming and archive building across 34th Avenue, opened in 2023 and includes a 75-seat performance space, an exhibition hall, and the public archive of Armstrong's papers, recordings, and personal effects.

## Music history

### 1943–1971 — Louis Armstrong's Corona residence

Louis Armstrong and his fourth wife, Lucille Wilson Armstrong, bought the 34-56 107th Street house in March 1943. Armstrong was 41, at the height of his swing-era fame; Lucille selected the modest two-story brick house specifically because she thought a working-class Black neighborhood was the right address for them, not the Sugar Hill / Harlem townhouses other Black entertainers of the period had moved into. The Armstrongs lived there for the rest of Louis's life. He died at home, in his upstairs bedroom, on July 6, 1971. Lucille kept the house unchanged until her death in 1983, willing it to Queens College — which opened it as the Louis Armstrong House Museum in 2003.

The 1943-to-1971 residency is technically inside this wiki's 1970s coverage frame on the trailing edge — Armstrong was active in Corona, recording, and traveling through the late 1960s and his final months. His 1968 *What a Wonderful World* recording sessions (commercially huge in the UK but not the US until later) were partly worked out at the Corona house with Joe Glaser's office logistics. The Armstrong residential biography is one of the most-documented in jazz: he left thousands of reel-to-reel tapes (including a remarkable archive of self-recorded conversations and home recordings), photographs, and personal papers, all preserved in the Corona house archive.

### 1980s — Kool G Rap and the Juice Crew

Nathaniel Wilson — Kool G Rap — was born in Elmhurst and raised primarily in Corona; he attended Newtown High School in Elmhurst-Corona before signing with Cold Chillin' Records through producer Marley Marl ([Queensbridge](../long-island-city/overview.md)) and DJ Polo as a duo around 1986. *Road to the Riches* (Cold Chillin', March 1989), *Wanted: Dead or Alive* (1990), and *Live and Let Die* (1992) made Kool G Rap & DJ Polo one of the central acts of the Juice Crew era — alongside Big Daddy Kane, Biz Markie, Roxanne Shanté, and Masta Ace, all on Cold Chillin' through Marley Marl's production. Kool G Rap's lyrical-density and gangster-storytelling approach prefigured much of what Nas and Mobb Deep would refine commercially five years later; his influence on East Coast rap craft is widely cited.

### 1990s–today — limited residential rap, no venue scene

Corona after 1990 has stayed off the residential rap map for English-language rap, though the neighborhood's Latino music geography is dense — *bachata*, *reggaeton*, and Mexican *banda* and *cumbia* circuits have been continuously active on Roosevelt Avenue and at neighborhood quinceañeras, weddings, and Roosevelt Avenue bars. The 7-train corridor's Latino-music geography is one of the city's denser regional-music environments but sits adjacent to this wiki's broader frame.

The 2023 opening of the **Louis Armstrong Center** added a programmed jazz venue to the neighborhood for the first time — Jason Moran (current Jazz at Lincoln Center artistic director for The Loft) curates an ongoing chamber-jazz series in the 75-seat performance space.

## Key venues

- **Louis Armstrong House Museum** — 34-56 107th Street; the preserved residence, the most-visited music-historic site in Queens, public museum since 2003.
- **Louis Armstrong Center** — 34-02 107th Street (across the street); the 2023 archive-and-performance building. Programmed jazz events, lectures, and exhibitions.

Corona has no general-purpose concert venue. The borough's flagship rooms ([Forest Hills Stadium](../forest-hills/venues/forest-hills-stadium.md), [Knockdown Center](../ridgewood/venues/knockdown-center.md)) sit elsewhere.

## Notable artists and acts

- Louis Armstrong — Corona resident 1943–1971; the most-documented jazz musician in American history.
- Kool G Rap — Corona; Juice Crew anchor, late-1980s Cold Chillin' canon.
- Lucille Wilson Armstrong — the choreographer, dancer, and Cotton Club Black-Broadway-era performer; not a primary figure in this wiki's frame but a part of the Armstrong-residence story.
- Tito Puente — born in Harlem but spent his later residential years in Manhattan and St. Albans; sometimes claimed by the Latino-Queens framing, less directly by Corona.

## Today

Corona in 2026 remains a predominantly Latino working-class neighborhood with the Louis Armstrong House and the new Louis Armstrong Center as the two visible music-institutional anchors. Real-estate pressure has been limited compared to Long Island City or Astoria; the neighborhood's residential music geography is principally Latin-American and largely off the press-canon map of this wiki. The Armstrong House and Center together have made Corona one of the most-visited music-tourism destinations inside Queens.
