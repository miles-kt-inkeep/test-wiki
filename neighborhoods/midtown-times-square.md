# Midtown and Times Square

Midtown stretches from 30th Street north to 59th, river to river, with the Theater District and Times Square clustered around Broadway from roughly 42nd to 53rd. It is the big-room half of Manhattan music: Madison Square Garden at 33rd and Seventh, [Studio 54](../venues/studio-54.md) at 254 West 54th, the Brill Building songwriter factory at 1619 Broadway, Roseland Ballroom at 239 West 52nd, and the Hammerstein Ballroom at 311 West 34th. Where the [Lower East Side](./lower-east-side.md) and [SoHo](./soho.md) supplied the scenes, Midtown supplied the stages — the rooms big enough and central enough to host the artists once they had broken through.

## Geography and character

Times Square through the 1970s and into the early 1990s was the grindhouse era: porn theaters, peep shows, three-card monte, and the cheapest stretch of midtown commercial real estate. That seediness is the backdrop for the disco and arena-rock years: [Studio 54](../venues/studio-54.md) in 1977 opened a few blocks from a 42nd Street that *Taxi Driver* had captured the year before. Sixth Avenue was corporate office; Seventh and Eighth were transient hotels and theaters; Ninth and Tenth were Hell's Kitchen tenements. The geography of the music scene tracked the corridor between the Garden at 33rd and Studio 54 at 54th — about a mile of Broadway and Seventh.

## Music history

The Brill Building at 1619 Broadway, between 49th and 50th, was the Tin Pan Alley successor that ran the early 1960s pop songwriting trade — Carole King, Burt Bacharach, Doc Pomus, and Neil Diamond all worked out of those offices. By the 1970s the songwriting industry had dispersed, but the building's myth set the template for what Midtown was: the music industry's office park.

Madison Square Garden, in its current 33rd Street form since 1968, is the city's defining arena. Elvis played his only New York concerts there in 1972; Led Zeppelin filmed *The Song Remains the Same* there in 1973; John Lennon's last full concert appearance was Elton John's MSG show in 1974. Every major act of every decade since has played the Garden.

[Studio 54](../venues/studio-54.md) opened in April 1977 in a former CBS television studio at 254 West 54th and ran for 33 months under its original Steve Rubell / Ian Schrager management before tax-evasion charges shut it down. In that short window it became the global capital of [disco](../genres/disco.md), with [Blondie](../artists/blondie.md), [Madonna](../artists/madonna.md), Bianca Jagger, Andy Warhol, Liza Minnelli, and Truman Capote among its regulars. Its closing in 1980 effectively marked the end of the disco era as a Midtown phenomenon.

Roseland Ballroom, at 239 West 52nd, ran as a mid-size concert venue through the 1990s and 2000s — [the Strokes](../artists/strokes.md), [Yeah Yeah Yeahs](../artists/yeah-yeah-yeahs.md), and a generation of indie acts played its 3,500-capacity room before it closed in 2014. Hammerstein Ballroom at 311 West 34th, in the Manhattan Center, has played a similar role since 1997.

## Key venues

- [Studio 54](../venues/studio-54.md) — 254 West 54th Street; disco's global capital, 1977–1980 in original form.
- Madison Square Garden — 4 Pennsylvania Plaza (33rd & 7th); the city's defining arena.
- Roseland Ballroom — 239 West 52nd; mid-size concert venue (closed 2014).
- Hammerstein Ballroom — 311 West 34th; mid-size venue inside the Manhattan Center.
- Brill Building — 1619 Broadway; songwriter factory of the 1960s.
- Radio City Music Hall — 1260 Sixth Avenue; the 6,000-seat showpiece.

## Notable artists and acts

- [Blondie](../artists/blondie.md) — Studio 54 fixtures during the late 1970s, after their CBGB years.
- [Madonna](../artists/madonna.md) — late-period Studio 54 regular before her own breakthrough.
- [The Strokes](../artists/strokes.md) — graduated from Bowery Ballroom to Roseland in the early 2000s.
- [Yeah Yeah Yeahs](../artists/yeah-yeah-yeahs.md) — multiple Roseland and Hammerstein dates through the 2000s.
- [Vampire Weekend](../artists/vampire-weekend.md) — repeatedly sold out Hammerstein and the Garden's smaller theater in the 2010s.

## Today

Times Square has been Disneyfied since the mid-1990s rezoning under Giuliani — the grindhouses are gone, replaced by chain restaurants and family theater. The Garden is still the Garden; Radio City and Hammerstein still book big tours. Roseland is gone, demolished in 2014 for a condo tower, which is the typical fate of Midtown music real estate. The neighborhood's role hasn't really changed: it is still where Manhattan music goes when it gets big enough to need 3,000 to 20,000 seats. The scenes themselves are still elsewhere — downtown, uptown, and across the river in Brooklyn.
