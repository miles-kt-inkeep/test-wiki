# Hollis

Hollis is the Southeast Queens neighborhood that, between roughly 1981 and 1988, produced the residential half of commercial hip-hop's first mainstream wave. [Run-DMC](../../genres/hip-hop/artists/run-dmc.md), [LL Cool J](../../genres/hip-hop/artists/ll-cool-j.md), Russell Simmons (Def Jam's co-founder), Ja Rule, and the early-90s indie-rap roster around Mic Geronimo all came up here, mostly within a half-mile of Hollis Avenue and 205th Street. The neighborhood's claim is straightforward: more multi-platinum 1980s rap acts grew up here, in tighter geographic proximity, than in any other neighborhood in the United States.

## Geography and character

Hollis sits in Southeast Queens between Jamaica to the south and Queens Village to the east, bounded roughly by Hillside Avenue to the north, Hollis Avenue running east–west through the middle, and 205th and 199th Streets running north–south as the music-historic spine. It is a low-density residential neighborhood of single-family houses and two-family attached homes — closer in built environment to suburban Long Island than to most of the city. The Long Island Rail Road has a Hollis station on the Main Line (the closest commuter rail stop to the residential blocks the rappers actually lived on); the F train at 169th Street (Jamaica) and the J at Jamaica Center are the most-used subways for getting out of the neighborhood. Demographically Hollis has been predominantly Black (with growing Caribbean immigration) through the period this wiki covers.

The compression of the music geography is part of the story. Run-DMC's Joseph Simmons grew up at 207-21 Hollis Avenue. DMC grew up at 197-30 Hollis. Jam Master Jay grew up at 90-31 203rd Street. LL Cool J's grandparents' house, where he wrote and recorded his early demos, was at 191-44 Murdock Avenue. Russell Simmons grew up at 205-29 111th Avenue. All five addresses sit inside a fifteen-minute walk of each other, with the corner of Hollis Avenue and 205th Street as the center of gravity — the same corner Run-DMC name-checks in "Christmas in Hollis" (1987) when "I was chillin' with my friends, on the corner — the corner, you know the corner, Hollis Ave."

## Music history

### Pre-1981 — middle-class Black Queens

Hollis through the 1960s and 1970s was a stable Black middle-class neighborhood — the parents of the rappers worked as transit workers, postal employees, teachers, and small-business owners. The Simmons brothers' father, Daniel, was a New York City Board of Education attendance supervisor; their mother, Evelyn, was a parks-recreation administrator and amateur painter. The pre-music story is one of the borough's most-cited examples of the second-generation Black middle class that the post-WWII Queens housing market produced.

### 1981–1984 — Run-DMC forms; demos and the Profile deal

The teenage Run, DMC, and Jam Master Jay started rehearsing together around 1981–1982 in the basement of Run's family's Hollis Avenue house. Russell Simmons (Run's older brother), already working as a rap promoter and Kurtis Blow's manager out of City College in Harlem, signed Run-DMC to Profile Records in 1983. "It's Like That" / "Sucker M.C.'s" came out as a single in March 1983 and reached #15 on the Billboard R&B chart. The self-titled debut album (Profile, May 1984) sold 500,000 copies and made Run-DMC the first rap act to go gold.

### 1985 — LL Cool J, Def Jam, and the platinum era

In May 1984 Russell Simmons co-founded Def Jam Recordings with Rick Rubin at NYU. A few blocks south of Run-DMC's house, on Murdock Avenue, the fifteen-year-old James Todd Smith — calling himself LL Cool J ("Ladies Love Cool James") — was recording demos in his grandparents' basement on a four-track. He mailed one to Def Jam's NYU address; Rubin and Simmons signed him a few weeks later. *Radio*, released November 18, 1985, was the second Def Jam album ever (after the various-artists *Def Jam Recordings: The First Year*) and the label's first solo platinum-certified release.

In 1986 Run-DMC's third album *Raising Hell* — including the Aerosmith collaboration "Walk This Way" — became the first rap album to go platinum and the first to crack the Billboard 200 top ten. By the end of that year, Hollis-rooted artists had cleared three industry firsts: the first gold rap album (*Run-D.M.C.*, 1984), the first platinum rap solo album (*Radio*, 1985), and the first platinum rap group album (*Raising Hell*, 1986). The neighborhood became, briefly, the most-photographed Black middle-class block in the country — *Tougher Than Leather* (1988), the Def Jam-produced Run-DMC film, used the actual Hollis houses as locations.

### Late 1980s–1990s — the Hollis Crew and the second generation

The Hollis Crew — Run-DMC's broader social circle including DJ Hurricane (Beastie Boys' DJ), Davy DMX, and several locally-known producers — anchored a roughly five-block independent-rap scene through the late 1980s. The Cold Crush Brothers played house parties in the neighborhood; Run-DMC's "Sucker M.C.'s" was a Hollis house-party staple before its commercial release.

In the early 1990s, Hollis produced a second wave. Mic Geronimo (signed to Blunt Recordings in 1994, *The Natural* in 1995) was an early collaborator with Ja Rule, DMX, and Jay-Z — the Hollis-Yonkers-Marcy axis that would dominate the late-90s commercial pop-rap moment. Ja Rule (Jeffrey Atkins, raised on Hollis Avenue) joined the Murder Inc. roster in 1998 and through the 2001–2003 commercial peak (*Pain Is Love*, *The Last Temptation*) was Hollis's principal commercial export of his decade. The 2002–2004 50 Cent feud — see [Jamaica](../jamaica/overview.md) — drew a generational line between Hollis and South Jamaica's commercial 2000s rap.

### 2000s–today — the Run-DMC memorial and Hollis as historical site

The 2000s closed Hollis's first-tier commercial period. Jam Master Jay was murdered in his Hollis recording studio at 90-10 Merrick Boulevard on October 30, 2002 — a killing that, like Biggie's and Tupac's, went unsolved for decades (federal charges were finally brought in 2020 and a conviction handed down in 2024). The Run-DMC corner at 205th Street and Hollis Avenue was renamed "Run-DMC JMJ Way" in 2009. The houses still stand; the neighborhood remains residential; the Hollis Famous Burgers restaurant on Hollis Avenue, opposite the Simmons house, has become an unofficial pilgrimage spot.

LL Cool J's Hollis residency ended in the late 1980s as his career commercialized; he kept the grandparents' Murdock Avenue address as part of his biography and frequently filmed video segments at the house through the 1990s.

## Key venues

Hollis is not a venue neighborhood — no club or concert room of any size sits inside it. Its musical importance is entirely residential. The 1980s house-party circuit ran through private basements and the public parks (Tony's, P.S. 192's yard, the basketball courts at Hollis Playground). The Jam Master Jay studios at 90-10 Merrick Boulevard, where Jay was killed in October 2002, operated as a private label-and-rehearsal facility rather than a public venue.

## Notable artists and acts

- [Run-DMC](../../genres/hip-hop/artists/run-dmc.md) — the founding act.
- [LL Cool J](../../genres/hip-hop/artists/ll-cool-j.md) — Def Jam's first platinum solo artist.
- Russell Simmons — Def Jam co-founder; from a hip-hop business perspective, the most consequential figure of his generation to come out of Hollis. His residential biography sits adjacent to but is not the focus of this wiki.
- Ja Rule — the late-90s / early-2000s commercial peak.
- Mic Geronimo — the under-known mid-90s indie-rap export.
- DJ Hurricane (Wendell Fite) — the Beastie Boys' DJ from 1986–1996, a Hollis Crew original member.
- Davy DMX (David Reeves) — the producer who supplied much of Run-DMC's early DMX-drum-machine sound; from Hollis.

## Today

Hollis in 2026 has gentrified slowly compared to most of New York. Property values have risen but the housing stock and demographic composition remain largely intact. The Run-DMC houses and corner are tourist-accessible but not commercialized. There is no dedicated music venue, museum, or commercial-historic site for the neighborhood's hip-hop legacy — a long-running proposal for a Run-DMC museum has not advanced beyond planning. Visitors who want to see the Hollis-of-record drive through residential streets and a few private addresses.
