# Bushwick

Bushwick is the Brooklyn neighborhood that, between roughly 2010 and 2022, became the city's primary warehouse-show district — the place every band and DJ played when the venue was an industrial loft on a lightly-zoned block. It absorbed most of the [Williamsburg](../williamsburg/overview.md) scene as Williamsburg rents climbed, and is now also the home of [Brooklyn Steel](./venues/brooklyn-steel.md), the Bowery Presents flagship that opened in 2017 and immediately became the city's most-booked 1,500–2,000-capacity room.

## Geography and character

Bushwick sits east of [Williamsburg](../williamsburg/overview.md) and north of [Bedford-Stuyvesant](../bedford-stuyvesant/overview.md), bounded roughly by the Queens border (Ridgewood) to the east, Flushing Avenue to the north, Broadway to the southwest, and Atlantic Avenue to the south. The L train (Morgan, Jefferson, DeKalb, Myrtle-Wyckoff stops) is the spine of music-relevant Bushwick; the Myrtle-Broadway J/M area is its southern anchor. East Williamsburg — the warehouse district between Bushwick proper and Williamsburg — is technically a separate planning name but is treated as Bushwick by anyone going to a show there.

The neighborhood is largely Latino (especially Puerto Rican and Dominican) and Black, with a layer of warehouse-loft and DIY-venue activity that arrived after 2008. Unlike Williamsburg the music geography here has remained genuinely industrial: the rooms are inside actual warehouses on actual industrial blocks, and the surrounding neighborhood is not a tourism scene. That's the reason the venues survive — the rents are still tolerable, and zoning has not yet pushed live music out.

## Music history

### Pre-2010 — almost no music story (in this wiki's frame)

Bushwick had vibrant Latin music nightlife and a long-running hardcore scene through the 1990s and 2000s, but in the indie-and-electronic frame this wiki is built around, the neighborhood was not a destination. That changed when Williamsburg's price floor began excluding bands.

### 2010s — DIY warehouse golden age

By 2012–2013 the eastern edge of Williamsburg and the western edge of Bushwick (the Morgan and Jefferson L stops) were the city's densest cluster of DIY venues. Roberta's pizza on Moore Street had become the unofficial neighborhood living room; Silent Barn (until its 2018 closure), Palisades, Body Actualized Center, Shea Stadium, and a long roll of un-named loft spaces ran shows almost nightly. The bands were largely the second wave of the post-2000 indie movement — Bushwick became the place you played before you graduated to Music Hall of Williamsburg.

The dance-music geography ran in parallel. Bossa Nova Civic Club opened in 2012 at 1271 Myrtle Avenue and became the neighborhood's defining small dance room — dark, foggy, no photos, techno and house programming. Mood Ring (2014) and other Latin-American-themed bars added to the circuit.

### 2017 onward — Brooklyn Steel and institutional Bushwick

[Brooklyn Steel](./venues/brooklyn-steel.md) opened in April 2017 at 319 Frost Street in East Williamsburg / Bushwick — a Bowery Presents project converting a 27,000-square-foot former metal-fabrication warehouse into an 1,800-capacity room. It immediately became the city's most-booked venue at that size, replacing both Manhattan rooms (Terminal 5 in midtown was significantly larger and less acoustically liked) and other Brooklyn rooms.

In the early 2020s, House of Yes (Wyckoff Avenue) extended its programming, and Public Records (technically Gowanus, frequently aggregated with Bushwick) became the city's most influential listening-room dance space. Knockdown Center sits across the Queens border in Maspeth/Ridgewood but functionally serves as Bushwick's largest semi-outdoor venue.

## Key venues

- [Brooklyn Steel](./venues/brooklyn-steel.md) — 319 Frost Street; the 1,800-capacity Bowery Presents flagship since 2017.
- Bossa Nova Civic Club (2012– ) — 1271 Myrtle Avenue; the defining small techno-and-house room.
- Elsewhere (2017– ) — 599 Johnson Avenue; multi-room concert venue and DJ space.
- House of Yes (2015– ) — 2 Wyckoff Avenue; performance-and-dance hybrid known for its theatrical programming.
- Silent Barn (2006–2018) — DIY space that anchored the early-2010s warehouse scene.

## Notable artists and acts

The Bushwick scene has been more venue-shaped than band-shaped. Most notable Brooklyn bands of the 2010s lived or rehearsed in Bushwick at some point but identified more strongly with Williamsburg or Brooklyn-at-large in their public framing. [Dirty Projectors](../../genres/post-punk-revival/artists/dirty-projectors.md) recorded portions of *Bitte Orca* in a Bushwick loft. The borough's 2010s second-wave indie bands — DIIV (Greenpoint/Bushwick), Beach Fossils, Frankie Cosmos, Parquet Courts (technically Ridgewood, frequently grouped) — drew heavily from Bushwick rooms.

## Today

Bushwick in 2026 has gentrified considerably but remains the most active warehouse-show district in the city. The combination of working industry, intact loft buildings, and an L-train-served indie population has been more durable than Williamsburg's equivalent. Brooklyn Steel anchors the institutional end; the DIY end is smaller than its 2014 peak but still active.
