# Lower East Side

The Lower East Side (LES) sits south of Houston Street, east of the Bowery, and north of the Brooklyn Bridge approach, bounded on the east by the FDR Drive and the East River. Tenement-dense and immigrant-built, it became the geographic cradle of New York [punk rock](../genres/punk-rock.md) in the mid-1970s and, a quarter-century later, the staging ground for the [post-punk revival](../genres/post-punk-revival.md) that briefly made downtown the center of rock music again. Its musical center of gravity has always been the Bowery — a strip of pawn shops, flophouses, and restaurant-supply stores that, against all odds, hosted the most influential rock club in American history.

## Geography and character

For most of the 20th century the LES was poor, loud, and architecturally stuck — six-story walk-ups, fire escapes, and a Bowery skid row that scared off real estate money well into the 1990s. That cheap rent is the reason the music happened here. Hilly Kristal opened [CBGB](../venues/cbgb.md) at 315 Bowery in 1973 because the neighborhood was practically free; a generation later, the [Mercury Lounge](../venues/mercury-lounge.md) and [Bowery Ballroom](../venues/bowery-ballroom.md) opened on Houston and Delancey for similar reasons. The blocks around Ludlow, Rivington, and Stanton Streets — once Yiddish theater country — became the late-1990s indie-rock corridor.

## Music history

In the mid-1970s, [CBGB](../venues/cbgb.md) became the launching pad for nearly every band that mattered in American [punk rock](../genres/punk-rock.md): the [Ramones](../genres/ramones.md), [Television](../artists/television.md), [Blondie](../artists/blondie.md), [Patti Smith](../artists/patti-smith.md), [Talking Heads](../artists/talking-heads.md), and the [New York Dolls](../artists/new-york-dolls.md) all played the room repeatedly between 1974 and 1977. The bands lived blocks away in the [East Village](./east-village.md), but the stage was on the LES side of the Bowery, and the LES claims the lineage.

Through the 1980s the Bowery stayed grimy and the punk scene faded into hardcore and a long CBGB matinee tradition, while [no wave](../genres/no-wave.md) and art-rock pulled toward [SoHo](./soho.md) lofts. The 1990s brought a slow flip: the Mercury Lounge opened on East Houston in 1993, the Bowery Ballroom followed at 6 Delancey in 1998, and a new generation of indie bands started showing up.

That generation broke commercially in 2001 when [the Strokes](../artists/strokes.md) released *Is This It* and made the LES the most photographed neighborhood in rock. [Interpol](../artists/interpol.md), [Yeah Yeah Yeahs](../artists/yeah-yeah-yeahs.md), and [LCD Soundsystem](../artists/lcd-soundsystem.md) circulated through the same blocks, gigging at Mercury Lounge and Bowery Ballroom on the way up. By 2006, when CBGB closed, the rents had already broken the scene's back.

## Key venues

- [CBGB](../venues/cbgb.md) — 315 Bowery; ground zero of New York punk (closed 2006).
- [Bowery Ballroom](../venues/bowery-ballroom.md) — 6 Delancey Street; the room where the post-punk revival broke.
- [Mercury Lounge](../venues/mercury-lounge.md) — 217 East Houston Street; small-room launchpad for nearly every 2000s indie band.
- [Max's Kansas City](../venues/maxs-kansas-city.md) — just north on Park Avenue South, but the LES/East Village punk scene treated it as home (closed 1981).

## Notable artists and acts

- [Ramones](../genres/ramones.md) — defined the CBGB sound; lived in the East Village but worked the Bowery.
- [Patti Smith](../artists/patti-smith.md) — held a famous residency at CBGB in 1975.
- [Blondie](../artists/blondie.md) — Bowery regulars before *Parallel Lines*.
- [Television](../artists/television.md) — Tom Verlaine's band that talked Hilly Kristal into letting rock bands play CBGB at all.
- [The Strokes](../artists/strokes.md) — 2001 breakout; their LES became the cover image of the revival.
- [Yeah Yeah Yeahs](../artists/yeah-yeah-yeahs.md) — Mercury Lounge graduates.
- [Interpol](../artists/interpol.md) — gigged Bowery Ballroom on the way to *Turn On the Bright Lights*.

## Today

The LES is unrecognizable. Boutique hotels line the Bowery, the New Museum sits across from the old CBGB address (now a John Varvatos turned Patagonia), and a one-bedroom on Ludlow goes for what an entire band paid in 1977. The Mercury Lounge and Bowery Ballroom still operate and still book good shows, which is more than most punk-era neighborhoods can say. The actual scene, though, moved across the river years ago — first to Williamsburg, then deeper into Bushwick and Ridgewood as Brooklyn rents chased it.
