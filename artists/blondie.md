# Blondie

Blondie is an American rock band formed in Manhattan in 1974 by singer Debbie Harry and guitarist Chris Stein, originating in the same [CBGB](../venues/cbgb.md) and [Max's Kansas City](../venues/maxs-kansas-city.md) circuit that produced [Ramones](ramones.md), [Television](./television.md), and [Talking Heads](./talking-heads.md). Where their peers stayed within the [punk rock](../genres/punk-rock.md) idiom, Blondie absorbed [disco](../genres/disco.md), reggae, girl-group pop, and even early [hip-hop](../genres/hip-hop.md), turning the Bowery scene into bona fide top-40 success. Harry's icy downtown glamour made her the visual face of late-'70s Manhattan music. The band has sold more than 40 million records worldwide.

## Formation and Manhattan context

Debbie Harry had previously sung in the folk-rock group Wind in the Willows and the proto-punk Stilettos before she and her partner Chris Stein founded Blondie in 1974. Early lineups churned through musicians until the classic group — Harry, Stein, drummer Clem Burke, keyboardist Jimmy Destri, and bassist Gary Valentine, later Nigel Harrison — solidified by 1976.

The band lived and rehearsed in lofts around the Bowery and [East Village](../neighborhoods/east-village.md), playing [CBGB](../venues/cbgb.md) constantly while making forays uptown to [Max's Kansas City](../venues/maxs-kansas-city.md) and later the disco rooms of [Studio 54](../venues/studio-54.md) and [Danceteria](../venues/danceteria.md). That fluency between the punk Bowery and the velvet-rope Midtown discos is the key to their sound.

## Sound and signature work

Their first two records, *Blondie* (1976) and *Plastic Letters* (1978), were straightforward punk-pop. Everything changed with *Parallel Lines* (1978), produced by Mike Chapman, which delivered "Heart of Glass" — a punk band openly playing four-on-the-floor disco — plus "One Way or Another" and "Hanging on the Telephone." It remains one of the great late-'70s pop records.

*Eat to the Beat* (1979) yielded "Atomic" and "Dreaming," and *Autoamerican* (1980) produced "The Tide Is High" (a reggae cover) and "Rapture" — the first No. 1 single on the Billboard Hot 100 to feature rapping, with Harry name-checking [hip-hop](../genres/hip-hop.md) pioneers Fab 5 Freddy and Grandmaster Flash. The 1999 reunion single "Maria" returned them to No. 1 in the UK, and 2017's *Pollinator* showed the act remained creatively viable.

## Manhattan venue history

- [CBGB](../venues/cbgb.md) — primary home base 1974–1977.
- [Max's Kansas City](../venues/maxs-kansas-city.md) — frequent bills with the Ramones and the [New York Dolls](./new-york-dolls.md).
- [Studio 54](../venues/studio-54.md) — Harry was a regular as both performer and scenester during the disco era.
- [Mudd Club](../venues/mudd-club.md) — a Tribeca hangout for Harry and Stein.
- [Danceteria](../venues/danceteria.md) — multiple sets and DJ-set appearances.
- [Bowery Ballroom](../venues/bowery-ballroom.md) — comeback-era New York shows from the late '90s onward.

## Legacy

Blondie was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2006 and remains active, with Harry, Stein, and Burke still touring. Their willingness to crossbreed [punk rock](../genres/punk-rock.md) with [disco](../genres/disco.md) and rap predated by decades the genre fluidity now taken for granted, and "Rapture" is routinely cited as the moment hip-hop crossed into mainstream pop. Debbie Harry is a permanent fixture in any narrative of '70s and '80s Manhattan style.

The band's downtown lineage runs directly to later New York acts including [Yeah Yeah Yeahs](./yeah-yeah-yeahs.md) and [The Strokes](./strokes.md), both of whom drew from Blondie's mix of attitude and pop instinct.
