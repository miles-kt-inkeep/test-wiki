# Television (band)

Television was an American rock band formed in Manhattan in 1973 by Tom Verlaine and Richard Hell, joined later by guitarist Richard Lloyd and drummer Billy Ficca. They are commonly credited as the band that physically opened up [CBGB](../venues/cbgb.md) for rock music — convincing owner Hilly Kristal in early 1974 to host original bands rather than the country, bluegrass, and blues acts the venue's name promised. Their 1977 debut *Marquee Moon* is among the most influential guitar records ever made, predating but conceptually adjacent to the [punk rock](../genres/punk-rock.md) explosion they helped enable.

## Formation and Manhattan context

Tom Verlaine (Thomas Miller) and Richard Hell (Richard Meyers) had been Delaware boarding-school friends who reunited in New York in the early 1970s and formed the Neon Boys with drummer Billy Ficca in 1972. The group reconvened as Television in late 1973 with the addition of guitarist Richard Lloyd. Verlaine talked Hilly Kristal into letting them play [CBGB](../venues/cbgb.md) on March 31, 1974 — the venue's first booking of an original-material rock band.

Hell left in 1975 (over creative tensions and Verlaine's refusal to record his songs) and was replaced by bassist Fred Smith, formerly of [Blondie](./blondie.md). The band lived and rehearsed across the [Lower East Side](../neighborhoods/lower-east-side.md) and [East Village](../neighborhoods/east-village.md). Their famous spring 1975 [CBGB](../venues/cbgb.md) residency with [Patti Smith](./patti-smith.md) is the moment the Bowery scene became visible to the press.

## Sound and signature work

*Marquee Moon* (1977) is built around Verlaine and Lloyd's interlocking lead guitars — closer to Quicksilver Messenger Service than to the [Ramones](../venues/ramones.md) — and the ten-minute title track is a masterclass in patient, modal guitar improvisation. The album also delivered "See No Evil," "Friction," and "Venus." *Adventure* (1978) was gentler and less celebrated; the band broke up in 1978.

A 1992 self-titled reunion album was warmly received but produced no comeback. Verlaine made a string of solo records that retained the patient guitar elegance of his band work. The "Marquee Moon" sound — long, clean Fender-into-Vox-AC30 guitar lines that argue rather than thrash — became a Manhattan house style passed down to [Sonic Youth](./sonic-youth.md), [Interpol](./interpol.md), and [The Strokes](./strokes.md).

## Manhattan venue history

- [CBGB](../venues/cbgb.md) — first booked March 31, 1974; effectively co-founded the venue's rock policy.
- [Max's Kansas City](../venues/maxs-kansas-city.md) — frequent late-'70s sets.
- [Bowery Ballroom](../venues/bowery-ballroom.md) — reunion-era shows in the 2000s and 2010s.
- The Bottom Line (Greenwich Village) — late-'70s headlining nights.
- [Mercury Lounge](../venues/mercury-lounge.md) — small reunion-era shows.

## Legacy

Tom Verlaine died in January 2023, ending any prospect of a full reunion. Richard Lloyd, Fred Smith, and Billy Ficca remain active. *Marquee Moon* is regularly placed in the top tier of all-time rock albums by critics. The dual-guitar architecture of the album — clean tones, no distortion, melodic lines that braid rather than blur — is the direct ancestor of the [post-punk revival](../genres/post-punk-revival.md) sound of the early 2000s and explicitly cited by [The Strokes](./strokes.md) and [Interpol](./interpol.md).
