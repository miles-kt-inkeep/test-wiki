# Electronic dance music in Manhattan

Electronic dance music in Manhattan is the through-line that connects 1970s [disco](../disco/overview.md) to garage, house, and the 2000s dance-punk of DFA Records. Its institutional spine runs from David Mancuso's Loft (1970) through Larry Levan's [Paradise Garage](../../neighborhoods/soho/venues/paradise-garage.md) (1977–1987) to clubs like Twilo, Sound Factory, and Body & Soul in the 1990s, and onto the dance-floor work of [LCD Soundsystem](../post-punk-revival/artists/lcd-soundsystem.md) and DFA in the 2000s. Manhattan's distinctive contribution to global dance music has been the DJ-as-auteur, the 12-inch single, and the racially and sexually mixed dance floor.

## Origins

Manhattan dance music's modern era begins with Mancuso's first "Love Saves the Day" loft party at 647 Broadway on Valentine's Day 1970 — an invitation-only, audiophile, predominantly Black, Latino, and gay event that established the template every subsequent Manhattan club followed. Through the early 1970s the model multiplied: Nicky Siano's the Gallery, the Sanctuary, the Continental Baths (where Frankie Knuckles and Larry Levan came up). When the [Paradise Garage](../../neighborhoods/soho/venues/paradise-garage.md) opened at 84 King Street in 1977 with Levan as resident DJ, it gave the post-disco "garage" sound a permanent home and a name.

After the Garage's closure in 1987, the lineage continued through Sound Factory (530 W. 27th Street, opened 1989) where Junior Vasquez built the next great residency, and through Twilo (1995–2001 in the same building), which hosted Sasha & Digweed's epochal Friday-night residency from 1997. The 1990s also produced the Body & Soul Sunday-afternoon party at Vinyl on Hudson Street (1996 onward, with François K, Joe Claussell, and Danny Krivit), explicitly designed as a Loft / Garage continuation.

The 2000s saw Manhattan dance music re-cross with rock through DFA Records (founded 2001 by James Murphy and Tim Goldsworthy on Greenwich Street), whose Rapture and [LCD Soundsystem](../post-punk-revival/artists/lcd-soundsystem.md) productions exported a Manhattan dance-punk sound to the world.

## Sound and aesthetic

Manhattan dance music has always been DJ-driven rather than producer-driven, which is why the city's signature is the long mix and the extended 12-inch edit (Tom Moulton invented the 12-inch single for a Manhattan-distributed Salsoul release in 1975). The "garage" sound that grew out of Levan's residency favored gospel-inflected vocals, live disco-funk basslines, and warm analog production over the colder drum-machine minimalism that Chicago house and Detroit techno would develop. DFA's 2000s output added post-punk guitars and cowbell to that template. The aesthetic — communal, multiracial, open until dawn, queer-positive — has been remarkably continuous from the Loft to Body & Soul.

## Key venues

- [Paradise Garage](../../neighborhoods/soho/venues/paradise-garage.md) — 84 King Street, Larry Levan's home from 1977 to 1987 and the namesake of "garage."
- [Studio 54](../../neighborhoods/midtown-times-square/venues/studio-54.md) — Midtown's celebrity disco, the genre's commercial peak from 1977 to 1980.
- [Danceteria](../../neighborhoods/chelsea-meatpacking/venues/danceteria.md) — Chelsea's multi-floor club where electro, new wave, and early hip-hop crossed.

## Key artists

- [LCD Soundsystem](../post-punk-revival/artists/lcd-soundsystem.md) — James Murphy's DFA project, the Manhattan dance-punk flagship of the 2000s.
- [Madonna](artists/madonna.md) — emerged from the post-disco club scene at Danceteria in 1982 and stayed close to Manhattan dance music for two decades.
- The DJ canon — Mancuso, Levan, Knuckles, Junior Vasquez, François K, Danny Krivit — whose work is the genre's actual authorial center.

## Neighborhoods

Manhattan dance music has lived mostly in [Chelsea / Meatpacking](../../neighborhoods/chelsea-meatpacking/overview.md) (Sound Factory, Twilo, Cielo), [SoHo](../../neighborhoods/soho/overview.md) and Hudson Square (the Loft, Paradise Garage, Vinyl), and [Midtown](../../neighborhoods/midtown-times-square/overview.md) (Studio 54).

## Decline / legacy

The Giuliani-era cabaret-law crackdowns of 1996–2001 closed many of the great rooms — Twilo lost its license in 2001 — and rising rents in Chelsea and the Meatpacking District replaced clubland with luxury hotels. EDM's 2010s commercial boom largely bypassed Manhattan in favor of festivals and Brooklyn warehouses. But the lineage isn't dead: Output (Brooklyn, 2013–2018), Nowadays in Queens, and a smaller Manhattan circuit at venues like Le Bain and the Brooklyn Mirage carry the Loft / Garage tradition forward, and Mancuso's "Loft" parties continued in various Manhattan locations until his death in 2016.
