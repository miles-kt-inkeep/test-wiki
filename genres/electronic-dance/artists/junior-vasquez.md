# Junior Vasquez

Junior Vasquez (born Donald Mattern, 1949) is the DJ whose Sound Factory residency at 530 West 27th Street from 1989 to 1995 carried the [Paradise Garage](../../../neighborhoods/soho/venues/paradise-garage.md) tradition into the post-Garage decade. After [Larry Levan](../../disco/artists/larry-levan.md)'s death and the closure of the Garage in 1987, Vasquez's Saturday-night sets at Sound Factory became the next great Manhattan dance residency — a 1,000-capacity, predominantly gay, predominantly people-of-color room where the production catalog of US garage / NYC house music was effectively built in real time.

## Formation and Manhattan context

Vasquez moved to New York from Pennsylvania in the 1970s, worked as a window dresser and a recording engineer at Shakedown Studios for [Arthur Baker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Baker_(musician)), and DJed at Bassline (a small early-1980s gay club) before being hired in 1989 by Richard Grant to open Sound Factory at the old Tunnel-adjacent warehouse at 530 West 27th Street. The room was explicitly designed as a Garage successor — Richard Long-engineered sound, predominantly Black and Latino gay clientele, no alcohol license, all-night-into-Sunday-afternoon hours.

His circle and apprentices through the Sound Factory years included [Madonna](./madonna.md) (a regular and a remix client), the production team at Strictly Rhythm Records (Vasquez was an unofficial A&R), Danny Tenaglia (his protégé who would become the next-generation Manhattan resident), and the New Jersey vocal-house community around Adeva and CeCe Peniston whose records he broke.

## Sound and signature work

Sound Factory sets ran from midnight Saturday until roughly 11 a.m. Sunday. Vasquez's signature was a heavier, more drum-driven and percussive sound than the gospel-disco hybrid that defined Levan's room — closer to Chicago house and to the breaks-led "tribal" style he helped popularize. The Sound Factory anthems are also Vasquez productions: "Get Your Hands Off My Man" by Junior Vasquez (1995), "If Madonna Calls" by Junior Vasquez (1996, a thinly veiled response to a Madonna business dispute), and a long catalog of remixes for Madonna ("Bedtime Story," "Secret"), Cyndi Lauper, Michael Jackson, Cher, and Whitney Houston.

His remix credit appears on more major-label dance singles in the 1992–1998 window than any other Manhattan DJ's, which is part of why the Sound Factory residency, more than any single record, is the institution that mattered.

## Manhattan venue history

- Sound Factory, 530 West 27th Street (1989–1995) — the defining residency. The club closed in March 1995 after a labor dispute with Richard Grant.
- Tunnel, 220 Twelfth Avenue (1995–1996) — Saturday-night residency in the year after Sound Factory's closure.
- Twilo, 530 West 27th Street (1996–2001, intermittent) — Vasquez had a Sunday-morning residency in the same building under the Twilo name; the room had been refit with a new sound system.
- Sound Factory Bar, 12 West 21st Street — distinct from the larger Sound Factory, the Bar was where [Frankie Knuckles](./frankie-knuckles.md) held his post-Chicago Manhattan residency through the 1990s.
- Pacha NYC, 618 West 46th Street (2005 onward, intermittent) — late-career Manhattan residency.

## Legacy

Vasquez is still actively DJing and has spent the post-2000s era moving between New York and Florida residencies. His Sound Factory years are widely treated as the last continuous Manhattan club residency to operate at the scale and influence of Levan's Paradise Garage — by 2001, Twilo's closure, the Giuliani-era cabaret-law crackdowns, and rising Chelsea real estate had ended the era of the year-round Manhattan-resident superstar DJ. The Body & Soul party (Danny Krivit, François K, Joe Claussell) carried the Loft / Garage day-party tradition into the 2000s; Vasquez's room was the last all-night-Saturday equivalent. His remixes and original productions remain a working catalog for any DJ digging through the New York vocal-house corpus.
