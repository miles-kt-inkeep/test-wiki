# Ornette Coleman

Ornette Coleman (1930–2015) was the Texas-born alto saxophonist and composer whose 1959 Five Spot residency in the East Village announced free jazz as a movement and whose Manhattan residence from 1959 to his death in 2015 made him one of the city's most consequential resident artists. The Prince Street loft he opened as Artists House in the late 1960s was an early prototype for the SoHo [loft jazz](../overview.md) network; his harmolodic theory shaped the post-1970 downtown improvisation scene; and his 1977 album *Dancing in Your Head* and 1995 album *Tone Dialing* reframed him repeatedly as a contemporary, not a historical, figure.

## Formation and Manhattan context

Coleman grew up in Fort Worth, played R&B in territory bands as a teenager, and developed his initial concept on the West Coast in the late 1950s with Don Cherry, Charlie Haden, and Billy Higgins. His arrival at the Five Spot Café in the [East Village](../../../neighborhoods/east-village/overview.md) for a six-week stand in November 1959 is the conventional starting point for the free jazz era; the band's run was extended to ten weeks under Atlantic Records' watch, and *The Shape of Jazz to Come* (recorded that year) became one of the most-discussed jazz LPs of the next half-decade.

He bought a building at 131 Prince Street in [SoHo](../../../neighborhoods/soho/overview.md) in the late 1960s and ran it as Artists House — performance space, residence, rehearsal hub — for several years before the lease economics ended that experiment. Through the 1970s he kept performance loft spaces in the area and toured intensively; in 1983 he opened a more lasting space called Caravan of Dreams in Fort Worth as a touring base, but New York remained his Manhattan home.

## Sound and signature work

Coleman's pre-Manhattan Atlantic catalog — *The Shape of Jazz to Come* (1959), *Change of the Century* (1960), *Free Jazz* (1961), the latter cut in two studios simultaneously — established the harmolodic vocabulary: pitch and rhythm liberated from chordal scaffolding, melody as the primary organizing principle. The mid-1960s Blue Note material with David Izenzon and Charles Moffett (*At the "Golden Circle" Stockholm*, 1965) and the trio with his son Denardo Coleman extended the project.

The pivot to electric harmolodics came with *Dancing in Your Head* (1977), recorded with Prime Time, his electric ensemble — two guitars, two basses, two drum kits — and continuing through *Of Human Feelings* (1979) and *In All Languages* (1987). The Pulitzer Prize for Music in 2007 (for *Sound Grammar*) capped a career-late critical re-evaluation that had been building since the 1990s. His Carnegie Hall 75th-birthday concert in 2005 — with Pat Metheny, Charlie Haden, Denardo, Geri Allen, and Joachim Kühn — was a defining late-period Manhattan event.

## Manhattan venue history

- The Five Spot Café (Cooper Square, [East Village](../../../neighborhoods/east-village/overview.md)) — the 1959 residency that introduced free jazz to New York.
- Artists House (131 Prince Street, [SoHo](../../../neighborhoods/soho/overview.md)) — late-1960s performance loft.
- [Knitting Factory](../../../neighborhoods/soho/venues/knitting-factory.md) — multiple appearances in the 1990s as the downtown scene reclaimed his lineage.
- Carnegie Hall — landmark 75th-birthday concert, 2005.
- Town Hall — multiple Prime Time and string-quartet concerts.

## Legacy

Coleman lived in Manhattan continuously from 1959 to his death in 2015 — a 56-year residency unmatched by almost any other major postwar jazz figure. His harmolodic theory, often dismissed as oblique in his own lifetime, became foundational vocabulary for [John Zorn](./john-zorn.md), Bill Frisell, the entire downtown M-Base wing, and the Brooklyn-based generation of Mary Halvorson, Tyshawn Sorey, and Vijay Iyer. His funeral at Riverside Church in June 2015 drew Sonny Rollins, Jack DeJohnette, Wynton Marsalis, and several thousand mourners; Denardo continues to manage and perform the catalog.
