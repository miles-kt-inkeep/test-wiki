# Grace Jones

Grace Jones (born 1948, Spanish Town, Jamaica) is the singer, model, and performance artist whose late-1970s disco run at Island Records made her a defining face of the [Studio 54](../../../neighborhoods/midtown-times-square/venues/studio-54.md) era and one of the few Manhattan disco artists whose post-disco reinvention exceeded her disco fame. She arrived in New York via Syracuse in 1968, was modeling in Paris by 1970, and returned to Manhattan in 1977 to record three Tom Moulton-produced disco albums for Island before pivoting to the Compass Point new-wave / dub records that would become her signature.

## Formation and Manhattan context

Jones moved to New York in the late 1960s, signed to Wilhelmina Models, and split her time between Manhattan and Paris through the early 1970s as one of the most-photographed Black models of the era — a regular in *Vogue* spreads alongside Jerry Hall and Jessica Lange. She signed to Island Records in 1977 and cut her first three albums — *Portfolio* (1977), *Fame* (1978), and *Muse* (1979) — with Salsoul producer Tom Moulton at Sigma Sound in Philadelphia, with promotion built almost entirely around the Manhattan club circuit.

By 1978 she was a fixture at [Studio 54](../../../neighborhoods/midtown-times-square/venues/studio-54.md), where her appearances — leather, geometric Jean-Paul Goude styling, performances with live tigers — were as much of the show as the records. Her circle overlapped tightly with Andy Warhol's Factory orbit, with Keith Haring (who would body-paint her for the cover of *Island Life*), and with [Madonna](../../electronic-dance/artists/madonna.md), whose "Material Girl" persona owes a clear debt.

## Sound and signature work

The Island disco records were classical Tom Moulton productions — strings, four-on-the-floor, extended 12-inch mixes. "I Need a Man" (1977), "La Vie en Rose" (1977 — her cover of the Edith Piaf standard), and "Do or Die" (1978) were dance-floor staples but not Top 40 hits in the U.S. The reinvention came in 1980 when Island sent her to Compass Point Studios in Nassau to work with Sly Dunbar and Robbie Shakespeare; the resulting "trilogy" — *Warm Leatherette* (1980), *Nightclubbing* (1981), and *Living My Life* (1982) — fused dub reggae, post-punk, and disco into something genuinely new. "Pull Up to the Bumper" (1981) was the breakthrough single and the bridge from her disco persona to her enduring artistic identity.

The Goude-directed visual catalog — the *Nightclubbing* cover, "Slave to the Rhythm" (1985), the Citroën CX commercial — is as central to her artistry as the music, and most of it was shot or conceived in Manhattan.

## Manhattan venue history

- [Studio 54](../../../neighborhoods/midtown-times-square/venues/studio-54.md) — the spiritual home of the late-1970s persona; her birthday parties were Studio 54 institutions.
- [Paradise Garage](../../../neighborhoods/soho/venues/paradise-garage.md) — Larry Levan-era PA appearances with the Compass Point material.
- The [Mudd Club](../../../neighborhoods/soho/venues/mudd-club.md) — downtown crossover during the disco-to-new-wave transition.
- Roseland Ballroom — multiple 1980s and 1990s tour stops.
- Madison Square Garden — One Man Show tour, 1981.

## Legacy

Jones never matched the U.S. commercial heights of her Compass Point peak, but her cultural footprint kept compounding: an acting career (*Conan the Destroyer*, *A View to a Kill*, *Boomerang*), a return album *Hurricane* (2008), and an enduring touring presence in her seventies. Her influence is most visible downstream — Janelle Monáe, FKA twigs, Rihanna's *Anti* persona — but the through-line is the Studio 54 / Compass Point fusion of [disco](../overview.md), Black diasporic music, and high-art performance that she basically invented. The 2017 documentary *Grace Jones: Bloodlight and Bami* is the canonical visual document.
