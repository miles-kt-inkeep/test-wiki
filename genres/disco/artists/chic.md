# Chic

Chic was the disco band founded in 1976 by guitarist Nile Rodgers and bassist Bernard Edwards in Manhattan, with drummer Tony Thompson rounding out the rhythm core. Across three years and four albums they produced some of the genre's most-covered records — "Dance, Dance, Dance (Yowsah, Yowsah, Yowsah)" (1977), "Le Freak" (1978), and "Good Times" (1979) — and built a production house at the [Power Station](../../../neighborhoods/midtown-times-square/overview.md) that outlasted disco itself, going on to shape early-'80s pop for [Madonna](../../electronic-dance/artists/madonna.md), David Bowie, Diana Ross, and Sister Sledge.

## Formation and Manhattan context

Rodgers and Edwards met as session musicians on the Apollo and Sesame Street circuit in the early 1970s and were playing together in the Big Apple Band when they pivoted to a disco concept after seeing Roxy Music live in 1976. Their first single "Dance, Dance, Dance" was cut at the [Electric Lady Studios](../../../neighborhoods/greenwich-village/venues/electric-lady-studios.md) overspill rooms and broke on Manhattan dance floors before national radio caught up. Atlantic Records signed them in early 1977.

The most-told Chic origin story is the "Le Freak" anecdote: turned away from [Studio 54](../../../neighborhoods/midtown-times-square/venues/studio-54.md) on New Year's Eve 1977 despite a personal invite from Grace Jones, Rodgers and Edwards went home and wrote a furious "Aaahh, fuck off!" hook that they later softened to "Aaahh, freak out!" The track went to number one on the Billboard Hot 100 in December 1978 and became Atlantic Records' best-selling single of all time.

## Sound and signature work

Chic's signature was Edwards' bass — popped, percussive, melodic — locked to Thompson's drums and Rodgers' chicken-scratch rhythm guitar (his "chucking" technique), with strings and horn arrangements by Gene Orloff layered above. The arrangements were spare and architectural rather than orchestral; every part had a defined role and pocket. That clarity is why the records still sample-clear today: "Good Times" became the bassline foundation for the Sugarhill Gang's "Rapper's Delight" (1979), Queen's "Another One Bites the Dust" (1980), and a generation of [hip-hop](../../hip-hop/overview.md) and dance records.

Studio albums *Chic* (1977), *C'est Chic* (1978), *Risqué* (1979), and *Real People* (1980) were all cut with Bob Clearmountain engineering at the Power Station on West 53rd Street. The Rodgers/Edwards production team also defined Sister Sledge's *We Are Family* (1979) — recorded in the same room with the same band — and produced records for Diana Ross (*Diana*, 1980) before disco's commercial collapse in 1980–81 forced them out of the Atlantic disco roster.

## Manhattan venue history

- [Studio 54](../../../neighborhoods/midtown-times-square/venues/studio-54.md) — the venue the band could not get into, and the namesake-by-inversion of "Le Freak."
- [Paradise Garage](../../../neighborhoods/soho/venues/paradise-garage.md) — Larry Levan's room where Chic 12-inches were canon.
- Power Station (West 53rd Street) — the production base for the entire 1977–80 catalog and many of the Rodgers-produced outside records.
- [Electric Lady Studios](../../../neighborhoods/greenwich-village/venues/electric-lady-studios.md) — early sessions before Power Station became the home base.

## Legacy

Chic's commercial peak ended with the disco backlash of 1980, but Rodgers' production career exploded immediately after — David Bowie's *Let's Dance* (1983), [Madonna](../../electronic-dance/artists/madonna.md)'s *Like a Virgin* (1984), and Duran Duran's *Notorious* (1986) all carry the Chic DNA. Bernard Edwards died in 1996; Tony Thompson in 2003; Rodgers reactivated the band in the 2010s and continues to tour. The Rock and Roll Hall of Fame nominated Chic eleven times before finally inducting them in 2017 in a special "Award for Musical Excellence" category — a delay widely seen as a residual rock-establishment grudge against [disco](../overview.md).
