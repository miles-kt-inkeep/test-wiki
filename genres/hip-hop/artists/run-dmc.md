# Run-DMC

Run-DMC is the [Hollis](../../../neighborhoods/hollis/overview.md), Queens trio of Joseph "Run" Simmons (born November 14, 1964), Darryl "DMC" McDaniels (May 31, 1964), and Jason "Jam Master Jay" Mizell (January 21, 1965 – October 30, 2002). Through the 1983-to-1992 commercial peak the group cleared an unprecedented stack of rap firsts: the first hip-hop act to go gold (*Run-D.M.C.*, 1984), the first to go platinum (*King of Rock*, 1985, eventually; and definitively with *Raising Hell*, 1986), the first to enter heavy MTV rotation, the first to perform at Live Aid (1985), the first to collaborate commercially with a rock band (Aerosmith, "Walk This Way," 1986), and the first to grace the cover of *Rolling Stone*. They are the canonical bridge from rap-as-Bronx-block-party form to rap-as-mainstream-pop-music; no other hip-hop act has the same density of "first" credits.

## Origins and Hollis geography

The three members grew up within a half-mile of each other in [Hollis](../../../neighborhoods/hollis/overview.md), a Southeast Queens neighborhood of single-family houses and two-family attached homes. Joseph Simmons (later Run) grew up at 207-21 Hollis Avenue; his older brother Russell Simmons (later Def Jam's co-founder) was nine years his senior and already working in the rap-promotion business in Harlem when Joseph was in middle school. Darryl McDaniels (later DMC) grew up at 197-30 Hollis Avenue, two blocks away. Jason Mizell (later Jam Master Jay) grew up at 90-31 203rd Street, about five blocks south of Run's house. All three attended local schools — Run and DMC met at St. Pascal Baylon Catholic School, Jay at the neighborhood public schools — and began informal DJ-and-rhymes practice in basements and parks around 1979.

The early formation ran through Russell Simmons's promotional infrastructure. Russell — by 1980 managing Kurtis Blow and running rap shows out of City College in Harlem — gave the teenage Joseph a starter role as a hype-man billed as "Son of Kurtis Blow." Joseph and Darryl began rehearsing as a duo through 1982; Jason Mizell joined as DJ in 1983. They named themselves Run-DMC after the rapping convention of pairing two letters-or-words with an initial.

## Key work

### *Run-D.M.C.* (Profile Records, May 1984)

The debut. Russell Simmons signed the group to Cory Robbins's Profile Records (independent, Manhattan-based) in 1983; the first single "It's Like That" / "Sucker M.C.'s" came out March 1983 and reached #15 on the Billboard R&B chart. The full album followed in May 1984. The production — sparse drum-machine beats from Larry Smith and Russell Simmons, leaving room for the call-and-response rapping that defined the early group — was a significant departure from the disco-band session-musician sound of earlier rap singles. *Run-D.M.C.* sold 500,000 copies and went gold in 1984, the first hip-hop album to do so.

### *King of Rock* (Profile, January 1985)

The bridge to mainstream rock crossover. The title track and "Rock Box" prefigured the Aerosmith collaboration of the following year. The album eventually went platinum on the strength of the *Raising Hell* halo effect.

### *Raising Hell* (Profile, May 1986)

The commercial peak. Produced largely by Russell Simmons and Rick Rubin (who had co-founded Def Jam by this point but was working with Profile on a one-off contract for Run-DMC), *Raising Hell* sold three million copies in its first year and four million total. "Walk This Way," recorded with Aerosmith's Steven Tyler and Joe Perry, was the second rap song to enter the Billboard Hot 100 top ten and the first into the top five (peaking at #4). The accompanying music video — shot in a sound-stage construction of two rehearsal rooms with the wall coming down between them — was the first rap-and-rock crossover video on MTV. "My Adidas," "It's Tricky," and "You Be Illin'" followed as singles.

The album made Run-DMC the first rap act to play arenas as the headliner of their own tour (the 1986 Raising Hell tour) and the first to land a Adidas endorsement deal — the famous Adidas Superstars Run-DMC wore on stage became one of the most-photographed shoe-and-band associations in pop culture.

### *Tougher Than Leather* (Profile, May 1988) and the late-1980s decline

The fourth album and the accompanying Def Jam-produced film (also titled *Tougher Than Leather*, directed by Rick Rubin) tried to extend the *Raising Hell* moment but landed at the wrong cultural beat — by 1988 N.W.A.'s *Straight Outta Compton* and Public Enemy's *It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back* had relocated rap's critical center of gravity, leaving Run-DMC's good-natured-rock-crossover aesthetic looking dated. The album went platinum but the critical reception was diminished; the group's subsequent *Back from Hell* (1990) and *Down with the King* (1993) reset their commercial trajectory.

### Jam Master Jay's murder (October 30, 2002)

Jason Mizell was shot and killed in his Hollis recording studio at 90-10 Merrick Boulevard on October 30, 2002. The killing went unsolved for two decades; federal charges were brought in 2020 against Ronald Washington and Karl Jordan Jr., and convictions were handed down in February 2024. The murder effectively ended the group — Run and DMC continued performing occasionally but did not record new material under the Run-DMC name.

## NYC venue history

Run-DMC's New York venue history runs across both Manhattan and Queens. The earliest performances were Hollis house parties and Queens park shows; by 1984 the group was playing the [Latin Quarter](../overview.md#origins) and [Apollo Theater](../../../neighborhoods/harlem/venues/apollo-theater.md) regularly. The 1986 *Raising Hell* tour played Madison Square Garden as part of a three-night New York stand. Through the 1990s and 2000s the group continued to appear at the Apollo, Madison Square Garden, and various tribute and benefit events.

## Legacy

Within the per-borough hip-hop frame this wiki is built around, Run-DMC is the foundational [Queens](../../../neighborhoods/queens.md) commercial group — every Queens rap act from [LL Cool J](./ll-cool-j.md) through [Nas](./nas.md) through Nicki Minaj has cited the group, the [Hollis](../../../neighborhoods/hollis/overview.md) blocks, or the *Raising Hell* commercial template as direct lineage. Within the broader genre frame, Run-DMC represents the first generation that made rap-as-mainstream-pop-music possible — every commercial rap album since *Raising Hell* has worked downstream of the platinum-selling, MTV-friendly, Adidas-endorsed precedent the group set. The Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inducted Run-DMC in April 2009; the National Recording Registry inducted "Walk This Way" in 2014.
