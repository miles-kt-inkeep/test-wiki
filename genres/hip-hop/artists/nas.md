# Nas

Nas (born Nasir bin Olu Dara Jones, September 14, 1973) is a rapper from the [Queensbridge Houses](../../../neighborhoods/long-island-city/overview.md) in [Long Island City](../../../neighborhoods/long-island-city/overview.md), Queens, whose 1994 debut *Illmatic* is, by widespread critical consensus, the most fully-realized rap album about a single neighborhood. Across 16 studio albums between 1994 and 2024, Nas has held one of the longest continuous critically-celebrated careers in hip-hop; his Queensbridge biography is one of the most-documented residential-rap stories in the canon.

## Origins and Queensbridge geography

Nasir Jones was born in Brooklyn to the jazz cornetist Olu Dara (born Charles Jones III in Natchez, Mississippi, 1941) and Fannie Ann Little Jones. His parents separated when Nas was very young, and his mother raised him and his younger brother Jabari (Jungle, later of the Bravehearts) at apartment 6F at 40-26 12th Street in the Queensbridge Houses — at the time the largest housing project in North America with about 96 buildings and 6,000 residents. Nas attended local public schools and dropped out in eighth grade; his musical and literary education came from his father (jazz musicians had been frequent visitors to the apartment), from rappers in the project (Marley Marl ran the in-project demos and parties through the 1980s), and from extensive self-directed reading.

The Queensbridge geography is woven through Nas's lyrics with a specificity that has no near-rival in the rap canon. "N.Y. State of Mind" names "Forty deuce" (42nd Street in Manhattan) but also "the 12th, near the L" — the 12th Street footbridge over the East River near the F train Queensbridge stop. "One Love" addresses an imprisoned friend; "Memory Lane (Sittin' in da Park)" names specific Queensbridge parks; "Halftime" describes block-level project life. The album as a whole is constructed around a five-block radius of the Queensbridge Houses.

## The path to *Illmatic* (1991–1994)

Nas began recording demos in 1991, attracting attention from Main Source's Large Professor (who lived adjacent to Queensbridge) and through him from MC Serch (of 3rd Bass) and Faith Newman (an A&R at Columbia / Sony). Serch served as the executive producer who got Nas signed to Columbia in 1992; his guest verse on Main Source's "Live at the Barbeque" (1991) had been widely-circulating among rap-industry insiders as the year's most-anticipated lyrical debut for two full years before the album dropped. The deal with Columbia took eighteen months to close; the album's recording stretched from 1992 to 1994 across multiple Manhattan studios (D&D Studios, Battery Studios, Unique Recording).

## Key work

### *Illmatic* (Columbia, April 1994)

Ten tracks, 39 minutes. Production by DJ Premier (from Gang Starr), Pete Rock (from Pete Rock & CL Smooth), Large Professor (Main Source), Q-Tip (A Tribe Called Quest), and L.E.S. — five of the most-celebrated rap producers of the era, each contributing one or two tracks. The album opens with "N.Y. State of Mind" (DJ Premier's signature beat from a Joe Chambers sample), runs through "Life's a Bitch" (with father Olu Dara playing the closing cornet outro), "The World Is Yours," "Halftime," "Memory Lane (Sittin' in da Park)," "One Love," "One Time 4 Your Mind," "Represent," and "It Ain't Hard to Tell" — and stops. Ten tracks. *Illmatic* did not sell platinum at release (it would over the following decade), peaked at #2 on the rap charts, and was met with universal critical acclaim that has only intensified.

The album's reputation has grown unusually steadily over the thirty years since release. *The Source* gave it five mics in 1994 (the magazine's highest possible rating, only given seven times in the magazine's history through 1994); *Rolling Stone*'s 2003 "500 Greatest Albums of All Time" list ranked it among the top 400; the 2020 update placed it at #44; the 2003 *Vibe* list of 51 greatest albums ranked it third. The album is the standard reference for "the definitive single-neighborhood rap album," a category that includes *Ready to Die* (Brooklyn), *The Infamous* (Queensbridge), and *2001* (Compton) as its closest peers.

### *It Was Written* (Columbia, July 1996)

The commercial follow-up. The Trackmasters production team (Tone and Poke) anchored the album's polish — "If I Ruled the World (Imagine That)" with Lauryn Hill and "Street Dreams" became major commercial singles. The album sold three million copies and made Nas, briefly, the most-paid East Coast MC in the genre. Critics split: some heard the polish as compromise (the album lost some of *Illmatic*'s street-level intimacy); others heard it as the natural commercial extension. The album has aged more controversially than *Illmatic*.

### Late 1990s — *I Am...*, *Nastradamus*, and the Esco / Escobar period

*I Am...* (Columbia, April 1999) and *Nastradamus* (Columbia, November 1999) extended Nas's commercial-mainstream period and were widely received as inconsistent. The "Esco" / "Escobar" persona — Nas's commercial-rap alter ego, a sharper-suited counterpart to the *Illmatic*-era street-poet voice — became a focal point of the period's criticism.

### *Stillmatic* (Columbia, December 2001) and the Jay-Z feud

*Stillmatic* opened with "Ether," a six-minute response to Jay-Z's "Takeover" (Roc-A-Fella, September 2001). The exchange — the "Ether" / "Takeover" feud — is widely considered one of the canonical rap-feud documents of the post-1996-Biggie-Tupac era. *Stillmatic* sold platinum and restored Nas's critical reputation; the feud with Jay-Z ended publicly in October 2005 with the two MCs sharing a stage at Power 105.1's "I Declare War" Madison Square Garden concert and Nas later signing to Def Jam (then under Jay-Z's presidency) in 2006.

### *Hip Hop Is Dead* (Def Jam, December 2006) through *Magic 3* (Mass Appeal / Universal, 2023)

Nas's third decade has been the most prolific of his career — ten studio albums between 2006 and 2024, all on Def Jam through 2018 and on his own Mass Appeal Records label after. The *King's Disease* trilogy (with Hit-Boy producing, 2020–2022) won the 2021 Grammy for Best Rap Album — Nas's first Grammy win after eleven nominations — and is widely considered his strongest late-career run.

## NYC venue history

Nas's early-career New York venue history runs through the standard 1993–1994 rap institutional set: the [Apollo Theater](../../../neighborhoods/harlem/venues/apollo-theater.md), the Latin Quarter, the Tunnel (the Chelsea club whose Sunday-night rap parties through 1995–2001 anchored the institutional New York rap scene), and various Manhattan and Queens park-jam events. The album-release performance for *Illmatic* in May 1994 was a small showcase at Wetlands Preserve in TriBeCa. Through the late 1990s and 2000s Nas regularly played Madison Square Garden as a headliner or co-headliner. The post-2010 New York performances have been concentrated on Mass Appeal Records events, the Brooklyn Hip-Hop Festival (annual since 2010), and various Queensbridge-anniversary and *Illmatic* anniversary performances.

## Legacy

Within the per-borough hip-hop frame this wiki is built around, Nas is the [Queensbridge](../../../neighborhoods/long-island-city/overview.md) / [Queens](../../../neighborhoods/queens.md) canonical MC — the artist whose work most fully realizes the "rap as residential-neighborhood document" tradition that runs from the Bridge Wars through Biggie's Bed-Stuy through Pop Smoke's Canarsie. Within the broader genre frame, Nas's *Illmatic* sits in the same critical position *Pet Sounds* occupies for rock or *Kind of Blue* for jazz — the perfect-album reference point against which other albums in the canon are measured. His continuous 1994-to-2024 career is one of the longest critical winning streaks in popular-music history.
