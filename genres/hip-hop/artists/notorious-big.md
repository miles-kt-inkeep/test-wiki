# The Notorious B.I.G.

The Notorious B.I.G. (born Christopher Wallace, May 21, 1972; died March 9, 1997) was a rapper from [Bedford-Stuyvesant](../../../neighborhoods/bedford-stuyvesant/overview.md), Brooklyn, whose 1994 debut *Ready to Die* and 1997 posthumous *Life After Death* made him the defining East Coast voice of 1990s [hip-hop](../overview.md). He is, by both critical and commercial measure, the most successful and most-quoted MC ever associated with Brooklyn, and one of the two or three most important rap artists in any frame of New York music history.

## Origins and Brooklyn geography

Christopher Wallace was born in St. Mary's Hospital in Brooklyn and raised by his mother, Voletta Wallace, at 226 St. James Place, on the Clinton Hill / Bedford-Stuyvesant border. He attended Queen of All Saints Middle School and George Westinghouse High School in Downtown Brooklyn, dropping out of the latter in 1989. By 17 he had been arrested twice for drug-related charges; by 19 he had recorded his first demos as Biggie Smalls. The producer / DJ Mister Cee passed the demo to *The Source*'s "Unsigned Hype" column in March 1992; Sean "Puffy" Combs (then at Uptown Records, soon to leave for Bad Boy) signed him later that year.

The geographic specificity is part of the artist's identity in a way that's atypical for major rap stars. Biggie's verses repeatedly name actual Bed-Stuy locations — Fulton Street, St. James Place, Bedford Avenue — and the iconography that built up around him after his death has stayed locked to those blocks. The mural at the corner of St. James Place and Fulton Street remains a pilgrimage site.

## Key work

### *Ready to Die* (Bad Boy, September 13, 1994)

The debut. The album opens with a hospital-birth skit and a *Mr. Magic* / *Schoolly D* / *Snoop Dogg*-tracking childhood-to-adolescence montage; closes with "Suicidal Thoughts," a single-take phone monologue. Between the bookends, *Ready to Die* arranged seventeen tracks into the most fully-realized rap-album-as-novel produced to that point. The singles — "Juicy," "Big Poppa," "One More Chance" — pulled the album to 4× platinum status; the deeper cuts ("Things Done Changed," "Everyday Struggle," "Suicidal Thoughts") gave it the literary weight critics still cite. Production was anchored by DJ Premier, Easy Mo Bee, and Combs's in-house team.

### *Life After Death* (Bad Boy, March 25, 1997)

Released sixteen days after Wallace's murder. A double album, broader in scope and more commercially ambitious than the debut — "Hypnotize," "Mo Money Mo Problems," and "Notorious Thugs" were the singles. The album debuted at number one and sold over 700,000 copies in its first week. The posthumous timing made it inseparable from the loss; the album has since been reframed by critics as the artistic peak of 1990s East Coast rap.

### Death and aftermath

Wallace was murdered in a drive-by shooting in Los Angeles on March 9, 1997, at 24 years old. The killing has never been definitively solved, though substantial reporting (Randall Sullivan's *LAbyrinth*; the FBI's reopened investigation) has tied it to the same tangle of West Coast / East Coast rap-industry, LAPD-corruption, and Suge Knight / Death Row Records contexts that produced Tupac Shakur's murder six months earlier. The combined weight of the two killings effectively ended the bicoastal rap-feud era of the mid-1990s.

## NYC venue history

Biggie's pre-fame Brooklyn footprint was block-party and street-corner — the recordings that became his first demos were made at 226 St. James Place and on Fulton Street corners. Once signed, his Manhattan venue history runs through the standard 1990s rap institutional set: the [Apollo Theater](../../../neighborhoods/harlem/venues/apollo-theater.md) for *Showtime at the Apollo* taping, mid-90s Latin Quarter bookings, in-store appearances at Tower Records on Broadway, and the broader Bad Boy promotional circuit. A 2005 documentary, *Notorious B.I.G. — Bigger Than Life*, mapped the geography in detail.

## Legacy

Within Brooklyn specifically, Biggie is the most-cited single artist in any frame of the borough's music history — the figure both [Jay-Z](./jay-z.md) and the present-day [Brooklyn drill](../../../neighborhoods/bedford-stuyvesant/overview.md#2010s-today--brooklyn-drill) generation reference as the canonical predecessor. Within rap broadly, his catalog and Tupac's together define the period and the rivalry that ended both their lives. Within the wider New York music story this wiki covers, he is the figure whose work and biography most clearly anchor the borough's claim to its own musical center of gravity through the 1990s, distinct from and parallel to the [Lower East Side](../../../neighborhoods/lower-east-side/overview.md) and [Harlem](../../../neighborhoods/harlem/overview.md) Manhattan-side scenes.
