# Jay-Z

Jay-Z (born Shawn Corey Carter, December 4, 1969) is a rapper, songwriter, and music-industry executive raised in the Marcy Houses on the southern edge of [Williamsburg](../../../neighborhoods/williamsburg/overview.md) — the Brooklyn project complex that sits a few blocks south of the Williamsburg Bridge and is treated culturally as part of the same Brooklyn rap geography as [Bedford-Stuyvesant](../../../neighborhoods/bedford-stuyvesant/overview.md). His 1996 debut *Reasonable Doubt* launched the longest-running solo career in New York rap, and the executive career that followed (Roc-A-Fella co-founder; Def Jam president 2005–2007; Roc Nation founder; Tidal acquirer; LiveNation partnership) made him the most successful music-industry figure New York has produced in the 21st century.

## Origins and Brooklyn geography

Shawn Carter was born in 1969 and raised in the Marcy Houses, a 1949-era public-housing complex bounded by Marcy Avenue, Flushing Avenue, Nostrand Avenue, and Park Avenue. The Marcy Houses sit in the planning-named "Williamsburg" but functionally between southern Williamsburg and northern Bedford-Stuyvesant; cultural identification has tended toward Bed-Stuy and Brooklyn-at-large rather than Williamsburg specifically, which by Jay-Z's adolescence in the 1980s was overwhelmingly Hasidic Jewish on the Bedford / Roebling side and not yet the indie-rock neighborhood it would later become.

Carter began rapping seriously in the mid-1980s, freestyling as "Jazzy" before settling on Jay-Z. He spent the early 1990s as a featured guest on Jaz-O and Big Daddy Kane records — the apprenticeship that turned him into one of New York's most technically-skilled MCs before his solo career started. He met Damon Dash and Kareem "Biggs" Burke in 1994; the three founded Roc-A-Fella Records in 1995 explicitly because no major label would sign Jay-Z to release the album he wanted to make.

## Key work — solo career

### *Reasonable Doubt* (Roc-A-Fella, June 25, 1996)

The debut. Self-released on Roc-A-Fella with distribution through Priority Records, the album was a critical success on release but a slow-burning commercial one — its initial chart peak was number 23. Over the next decade, as Jay-Z's commercial career grew, *Reasonable Doubt*'s reputation rose to the point of being treated by hip-hop critics as one of the strongest debut albums in any era of rap. The single "Can't Knock the Hustle," the duet with [The Notorious B.I.G.](./notorious-big.md) on "Brooklyn's Finest," and the closer "Regrets" carry the album's strongest writing.

### *Vol. 2... Hard Knock Life* (Roc-A-Fella, September 29, 1998)

The commercial breakthrough. The Annie-sampling title track, the Timbaland-produced "Nigga What, Nigga Who," and "Money, Cash, Hoes" pushed the album to 5× platinum and made Jay-Z one of the highest-selling rappers of the year. The Hard Knock Life Tour (1999, with DMX, Method Man, Redman, and DJ Clue) was the highest-grossing rap tour to that point.

### *The Blueprint* (Roc-A-Fella, September 11, 2001)

Released on the morning of the September 11 attacks. The album's first-week sales (over 420,000) and critical consensus — driven by Kanye West and Just Blaze's chipmunk-soul production, "Takeover"'s assault on Nas and Mobb Deep, and "Izzo (H.O.V.A.)"'s Jackson 5 sample — made it the consensus peak of Jay-Z's solo work and the launch pad for Kanye's own production career.

### Subsequent solo work

*The Black Album* (2003) was framed at release as Jay-Z's retirement from rapping; he returned three years later. *American Gangster* (2007), *The Blueprint 3* (2009), the *Watch the Throne* collaboration with Kanye West (2011), *Magna Carta Holy Grail* (2013), and *4:44* (2017) extend the catalog into a genuinely 30-year-plus solo career — unprecedented for any New York rapper.

## Executive career

Roc-A-Fella's commercial growth in the late 1990s gave Jay-Z standing in the music industry that no Brooklyn rapper had previously held. He sold Roc-A-Fella to Def Jam in 2004; Def Jam appointed him president of the label in 2005, a role he held for two years (signing Rihanna, Kanye West, and Ne-Yo to the label during his tenure). After leaving Def Jam he founded Roc Nation in 2008 (a management and entertainment company), Roc Nation Sports in 2013, and acquired Tidal in 2015. His 2008 marriage to Beyoncé Knowles created the most-watched music-industry partnership of the 21st century.

## NYC venue history

Pre-fame, Jay-Z's Brooklyn venue footprint was largely block-party, project-courtyard, and freestyle-cipher. His first major Manhattan presence was through the Roc-A-Fella offices at 175 Varick Street ([Greenwich Village](../../../neighborhoods/greenwich-village/overview.md)) from 1995 onward. Career-shaping NYC stages have included [Madison Square Garden](../../../neighborhoods/midtown-times-square/overview.md) (multiple sold-out residencies, beginning with the 2003 retirement show), Radio City Music Hall, and Barclays Center in Prospect Heights — Jay-Z partnered with Bruce Ratner on the Brooklyn Nets / Barclays development from 2003 to 2013, a partnership that put his name on the borough's most-watched arena.

## Legacy

Jay-Z's primary historical importance to New York music covers three intertwined arcs: as the Brooklyn rapper who, alongside [The Notorious B.I.G.](./notorious-big.md), defined the East Coast 1990s; as the artist whose catalog runs intact across three decades when most of his peers' did not; and as the executive whose career arc — from project-bred MC to billionaire industry figure — re-mapped what was possible for a New York rapper. Within the Brooklyn frame specifically, his Marcy Houses origin story and his return to the borough via the Barclays Center / Brooklyn Nets investment closed a loop that no other New York rap figure has matched.
