# No Wave

No Wave was a short-lived, deliberately abrasive Manhattan music and film scene that ran from roughly 1977 to 1982, concentrated in [SoHo](../../neighborhoods/soho/overview.md) lofts and [Lower East Side](../../neighborhoods/lower-east-side/overview.md) clubs. The movement defined itself against the increasingly polished [new wave](../new-wave/overview.md) breaking out of [punk](../punk-rock/overview.md), favoring atonality, noise, and confrontation over hooks. Its key venues were [Max's Kansas City](../../neighborhoods/greenwich-village/venues/maxs-kansas-city.md), the [Mudd Club](../../neighborhoods/soho/venues/mudd-club.md) at 77 White Street, and the loft scene around Artists Space.

## Origins

The movement is usually dated to the May 1978 "Artists Space" festival on Hudson Street, where Brian Eno saw four of the scene's bands — Mars, DNA, Teenage Jesus and the Jerks, and the Contortions — and decided to compile them. The resulting LP, *No New York* (Antilles, November 1978), produced by Eno, became the scene's founding document. Lydia Lunch (Teenage Jesus), James Chance (Contortions), Arto Lindsay (DNA), and Glenn Branca (Theoretical Girls) were its core figures.

No Wave overlapped heavily with the SoHo visual-art world and with the Cinema of Transgression. Filmmakers like Beth and Scott B and Vivienne Dick shot 8mm shorts on the same blocks where the bands rehearsed. The young [Sonic Youth](artists/sonic-youth.md) emerged at the tail end of this milieu in 1981, drawing directly on Branca's mass-guitar ensembles.

## Sound and aesthetic

No Wave rejected almost everything punk still kept: blues structure, recognizable chords, rock'n'roll rhythm. Teenage Jesus songs were under two minutes of detuned slide guitar and shrieked vocals; the Contortions grafted free-jazz saxophone onto skronky James Brown funk; DNA played angular, syncopated miniatures; Mars dissolved rhythm entirely. Performances were often physically hostile — Lunch and Chance both made a habit of insulting or assaulting the audience. The aesthetic was deliberately ugly, anti-virtuosic, and short.

## Key venues

- [Max's Kansas City](../../neighborhoods/greenwich-village/venues/maxs-kansas-city.md) — hosted many early no wave bills in 1977–78.
- [Mudd Club](../../neighborhoods/soho/venues/mudd-club.md) — 77 White Street, Tribeca, the scene's late-70s social headquarters.
- [CBGB](../../neighborhoods/lower-east-side/venues/cbgb.md) — staged the Contortions and Teenage Jesus alongside punk acts.

## Key artists

- [Sonic Youth](artists/sonic-youth.md) — emerged from the Branca ensemble in 1981 and carried the no wave guitar vocabulary into the indie era.
- Lydia Lunch / Teenage Jesus and the Jerks — the scene's most uncompromising voice, captured on *No New York*.
- James Chance and the Contortions — fused free jazz with punk on *Buy* (1979).
- Glenn Branca — composer whose massed-guitar symphonies bridged no wave and new music.

## Neighborhoods

No wave lived in [SoHo](../../neighborhoods/soho/overview.md) lofts, Tribeca around the Mudd Club, and the [Lower East Side](../../neighborhoods/lower-east-side/overview.md), where rents were still cheap enough to let entire bands share a single floor.

## Decline / legacy

By 1982 most of the original no wave bands had broken up, and the surviving figures (Lindsay, Branca, Lunch) had moved toward jazz, composition, or solo work. The scene was too inhospitable to sustain itself commercially, but it left an outsized influence: [Sonic Youth](artists/sonic-youth.md) carried its dissonance into college rock, Swans and Live Skull picked up its violence, and the international noise-rock underground of the 1990s (Boredoms, Boris, Lightning Bolt) is unimaginable without it. *No New York* remains in print on reissue.
