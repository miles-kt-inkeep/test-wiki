# NYC Music History (1970s–Present)

A wiki tracing the music made in New York City from the early 1970s through today — the scenes that were born here, the rooms where they happened, the artists who shaped them, and the neighborhoods that gave each scene its address. Two boroughs do most of the work: **Manhattan** held the historical center of gravity through the 1990s; **Brooklyn** has held it ever since.

> **Sourcing note.** This wiki is built from widely-documented music history (the canon of NYC music coverage in books, magazines, and Wikipedia). Each article ends with a "Further reading" section pointing to authoritative external coverage. Where a specific claim is contested or uncertain it is marked inline `(TODO: needs source)`.

## Eras at a glance

### Early 1970s — the comedown

The [Velvet Underground](./genres/punk-rock/artists/new-york-dolls.md) had broken up, [Greenwich Village](./neighborhoods/greenwich-village/overview.md) folk had aged into singer-songwriterism, and the city was bankrupt. Two reactions formed in Manhattan: glam-trash rock at [Max's Kansas City](./neighborhoods/greenwich-village/venues/maxs-kansas-city.md) (the [New York Dolls](./genres/punk-rock/artists/new-york-dolls.md)) and a stripped-down rock-of-amateurs scene at a Bowery dive called [CBGB](./neighborhoods/lower-east-side/venues/cbgb.md). Brooklyn at this point was almost entirely off the music map — its waterfronts industrial, its brownstones cheap but unfashionable.

### Mid–late 1970s — punk, no wave, disco, hip-hop's birth

[Punk rock](./genres/punk-rock/overview.md) coalesced at [CBGB](./neighborhoods/lower-east-side/venues/cbgb.md) around the [Ramones](./genres/punk-rock/artists/ramones.md), [Television](./genres/punk-rock/artists/television.md), [Patti Smith](./genres/punk-rock/artists/patti-smith.md), [Blondie](./genres/punk-rock/artists/blondie.md), and [Talking Heads](./genres/punk-rock/artists/talking-heads.md). Downtown art-rock turned into [no wave](./genres/no-wave/overview.md). Uptown and downtown both danced: [disco](./genres/disco/overview.md) ruled at [Studio 54](./neighborhoods/midtown-times-square/venues/studio-54.md) and the [Paradise Garage](./neighborhoods/soho/venues/paradise-garage.md). [Hip-hop](./genres/hip-hop/overview.md) was born in the Bronx and pulled into Manhattan within a few years.

### 1980s — clubs, cocaine, art-rock, rap crossover

[Mudd Club](./neighborhoods/soho/venues/mudd-club.md) and [Danceteria](./neighborhoods/chelsea-meatpacking/venues/danceteria.md) blended punk, art, hip-hop, and dance. [Madonna](./genres/electronic-dance/artists/madonna.md) used Danceteria as a launching pad. [Sonic Youth](./genres/no-wave/artists/sonic-youth.md) emerged from the no-wave hangover. The [Beastie Boys](./genres/hip-hop/artists/beastie-boys.md) crossed from hardcore to rap. [Hip-hop](./genres/hip-hop/overview.md) went mainstream. Across the river, [Bedford-Stuyvesant](./neighborhoods/bedford-stuyvesant/overview.md) and the Marcy Houses in southern [Williamsburg](./neighborhoods/williamsburg/overview.md) were producing the next generation of MCs — work that would surface as [The Notorious B.I.G.](./genres/hip-hop/artists/notorious-big.md) and [Jay-Z](./genres/hip-hop/artists/jay-z.md) by the mid-1990s. Real estate began rewriting the map.

### 1990s — alt-rock, hip-hop's golden era, the rent goes up

The [Knitting Factory](./neighborhoods/soho/venues/knitting-factory.md) and a maturing [Mercury Lounge](./neighborhoods/lower-east-side/venues/mercury-lounge.md) hosted indie rock and downtown jazz. The [Lower East Side](./neighborhoods/lower-east-side/overview.md) became the city's de-facto rock neighborhood. In Brooklyn, hip-hop's golden age ran in parallel — Biggie's *Ready to Die* (1994) and Jay-Z's *Reasonable Doubt* (1996) put [Bedford-Stuyvesant](./neighborhoods/bedford-stuyvesant/overview.md) and the Marcy Projects on the global rap map. Williamsburg's first warehouse parties and rooftop shows began drifting north from a still-mostly-industrial waterfront.

### 2000s — post-punk revival, Brooklyn arrives

[The Strokes](./genres/post-punk-revival/artists/strokes.md), [Interpol](./genres/post-punk-revival/artists/interpol.md), [Yeah Yeah Yeahs](./genres/post-punk-revival/artists/yeah-yeah-yeahs.md), [LCD Soundsystem](./genres/post-punk-revival/artists/lcd-soundsystem.md), and [TV on the Radio](./genres/post-punk-revival/artists/tv-on-the-radio.md) brought a [post-punk revival](./genres/post-punk-revival/overview.md) out of [Lower East Side](./neighborhoods/lower-east-side/overview.md) bars *and* Brooklyn lofts — TV on the Radio's *Desperate Youth, Blood Thirsty Babes* (2004) was tracked entirely in [Williamsburg](./neighborhoods/williamsburg/overview.md). The [Bowery Ballroom](./neighborhoods/lower-east-side/venues/bowery-ballroom.md) and [Mercury Lounge](./neighborhoods/lower-east-side/venues/mercury-lounge.md) were the proving grounds; by 2008 [Grizzly Bear](./genres/post-punk-revival/artists/grizzly-bear.md), [Dirty Projectors](./genres/post-punk-revival/artists/dirty-projectors.md), and the rest of the Brooklyn indie wave had relocated the conversation to the East River's far bank.

### 2010s–today — Brooklyn dominant, Manhattan as office

Manhattan's role shifts. [Vampire Weekend](./genres/post-punk-revival/artists/vampire-weekend.md) carries the indie torch out of Columbia. [A$AP Mob](./genres/hip-hop/artists/asap-mob.md) anchors a new [Harlem](./neighborhoods/harlem/overview.md) hip-hop wave. Most Manhattan rooms close; some (CBGB) become retail, others (Mercury Lounge, Bowery Ballroom, Blue Note, Village Vanguard) keep going. Brooklyn becomes the city's actual music neighborhood — [Brooklyn Steel](./neighborhoods/bushwick/venues/brooklyn-steel.md), [Music Hall of Williamsburg](./neighborhoods/williamsburg/venues/music-hall-of-williamsburg.md), [Saint Vitus](./neighborhoods/greenpoint/venues/saint-vitus.md), and a long tail of warehouses in Bushwick, East Williamsburg, and Ridgewood take the live-music load. Brooklyn drill emerges out of [Bedford-Stuyvesant](./neighborhoods/bedford-stuyvesant/overview.md) and Canarsie around 2019, briefly making Pop Smoke the most-watched young rapper in America before his death. Manhattan still sets the historical clock; Brooklyn now sets the present-day one.
